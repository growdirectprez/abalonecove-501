Petitioner / Plaintiff Community of Abalone Cove ("Abalone Cove") opposes the demurrer filed by Respondents / Defendants the City of Rancho Palos Verdes and the City Council of Rancho Pales Verdes (together, "the City"), as follows:

### Summary of Opposition

The City amended its municipal code on June 18, 2024. This amendment upzoned a lot located adjacent to Abalone Cove from single-family to high-density, multi-family housing. And--in the City's words-- "superseded" previous, identical zoning ordinances adopted via urgency procedures. Within the statutorily required ninety days, Abalone Cove petitioned the Court to invalidate the June 18 amendment. (Gov. Code, section 65860, subd. (b)(2) [giving interested parties 90 days to "bring an action" regarding the "amendment of any existing zoning ordinance"].)

The City concedes these facts. Despite this, it asks the Court to dismiss the Petition because Abalone Cove did not *also* challenge the "*superseded*" identical amendments passed two months and two weeks earlier, respectively. In its demurrer, the City argues that when it amends its municipal code with an identical ordinance that supersedes a prior ordinance, a petitioner must *also* challenge the earlier, superseded ordinance. This nonsensical position would preempt the statute of limitations proscribed by Government Code section 65860 by artificially compressing the timeframe to one set instead by the date of passage of an already "superseded" ordinance which (inexplicably) remains "still in effect" according to the City.

The City offers no applicable legal authority to support this argument. And there is none. The City is preempted from truncating the statutory rights of community members to challenge a zoning ordinance. When the City amended its municipal code on June 18, 2024, "the last amendment repeal[ed] the first" and the latter became the only "operative" ordinance as soon as it went into effect. (*Garber v. Levit* (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th Supp. 1, 6.) At that point, any challenge to the prior "superseded" ordinances became moot. Abalone Cove would have wasted judicial resources (and its own) by challenging all three. Instead, it attacked the sole operative ordinance within the 90-day statute of limitations.

The Court should deny the City's demurrer on the first four causes of action, which it bases on a lack of "subject matter jurisdiction," and thereby reject the City's attempt to shorten the statute

### I. Facts

#### A. Until 2024, the Clipper Lot was reserved for single-family use as a sensitive coastal area with minimal commercial development.

The Community of Abalone Cove is a homeowners' association made up of eighty homeowners in a single-family home community located near Abalone Cove in Rancho Palos Verdes, California, a city of about 40,000 residents. (Petition, paragraph 4.) Abutting the Community is an empty lot surrounded almost entirely by single-family residences. (*Id.*, paragraphs 24-26.) The empty lot--known as the "Clipper Lot"--sits in the City's Coastal Zone, an "extreme geological hazard area," within a half mile of the Portuguese Bed / Abalone Cove Landslide Complex, and a natural vegetation and wildlife corridor and FEMA flood zone. (*Id.*, paragraphs 27-30.) The Clipper Lot sits in the middle of a low-density, ecologically fragile, residential coastal community. (*Id.*, paragraph 25.)

#### B. The City revises its Housing Element to upzone the Clipper Lot to maximum residential density, despite its unfitness for development and lack of impact on affordable housing.

The City of Rancho Pales Verdes receives from the state and county government an allocation of housing units that it must construct to ensure an adequate supply of housing. (Petition, paragraphs 16-17.) The City then adopts and submits plans for this new housing for the state to approve, deny, or request modifications. (*Id.* paragraph 19.) This proposal is called the City's "Housing Element," which the City passed in March 2024 after revisions required by the state. (*Id.*, paragraph 21.)

The City's most recent Housing Element increased the density of the Clipper Lot from R-4 to RM-22, the maximum residential density in the City. (Petition, paragraphs 34-36.) RM-22 allows for five times the density of RM-4, essentially mandating that the lot contain a planned unit development (e.g., condos or townhomes) and the negative geological burden and impact on ecology and established land use patterns that necessarily come with it. (*Id.*, paragraphs 37-39.)

### III. Legal Standard

The only issue the court may resolve on a demurrer is whether the complaint, standing alone, states a cause of action under any possible legal theory. (*Gervase v. Superior Court* (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1218, 1224.) "[A]ll material facts pleaded in the complaint and those that arise by reasonable implication, but not conclusions of fact or law, are deemed admitted by the demurring party. The complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded." (*Rodas v. Spiegel* (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 513, 517 [citations omitted].)

### IV. Argument

In demurring to the allegations of Abalone Cove, Defendant resorts to a series of meritless procedural and technical objections, all of which are unfounded and may be easily rejected.

#### A. "Mootness" is not a basis to challenge this Court's subject matter jurisdiction.

The City argues that this Court "lacks jurisdiction over" causes of action 1-4 because they are "moot." (Demurrer, at p. 7 [relying on Code Civ. Proc., section 430.10, subd. (a)] ["The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading."].) But the City cites no case law or other basis for the use of mootness as a jurisdictional challenge, and relevant authority shows that "mootness" is not a proper basis to challenge jurisdiction.

#### 1. Subject matter jurisdiction exists unless the Court has no authority whatsoever.

Subject matter jurisdiction "relates to the inherent authority of the court involved to deal with the case or matter before it." (*Brown v. Desert Christian Center* (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 733, 740 [citations and quotations omitted].) A court has a "lack of subject matter jurisdiction" only when there is an "entire absence of power to hear or determine a case." (*Miller-Leigh LLC v. Henson* (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 1143, 1148-1149.)

#### 2. The City cites authority that supports the Court has subject matter jurisdiction.

The City relies on two cases, neither of which support its assertion that this Court can dismiss this case for lack of jurisdiction based on mootness. First it cites *In re D.P.*, a family law case involving a juvenile with no applicability to a civil court. *In re D.P.* discussed the mootness of an appeal (not an underlying suit), and that, in fact, undermines the City's position. (Demurrer at pp. 16:6-12 [citing *In re D.P.* (2023) 14 Cal.5th 266, 276].)

There, the jurisdictional issue was not a civil court's subject matter jurisdiction, but the jurisdiction of the court over a juvenile because a child has suffered or risks suffering physical harm. (*Id.*, at p. 273.) A juvenile court had determined it had jurisdiction over the child under the Welfare and Institutions Code; the parents appealed; and while on appeal, the juvenile court terminated its jurisdiction over the juvenile. (*Id.*) The California Supreme Court acknowledged that the parents'

appeal was "moot because Father ... has not demonstrated a specific legal or practical consequence" that would stem from a ruling. (*Id.*) But the Court did not dismiss the case for lack of jurisdiction: it held instead that "the Court of Appeal has discretion to review the case **even though it is moot**." (*Id.*, at p. 273 [emphasis added].) If anything, this case reflects that mootness is not a recognized basis to challenge subject matter jurisdiction.

The City also cites *Wozniak v. Lucutz* for the claim that "the Court lacks jurisdiction when ... the Court has no power to provide effective relief." (Demurrer, at p. 10 [citing *Wozniak* (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1040].) But that case does not even contain the words "moot" or "mootness," let alone state that a court lacks jurisdiction based on mootness. In fact, the Court states that it may have "jurisdiction over the subject matter" even if "it has no power ... to give certain kinds of relief." (*Id.*, at p. 1040.) *Wosniak* directly undermines the City's claim that a court can dismiss a case for "mootness" because it "has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading" (the grounds of the City's demurrer under C.C.P. section 430.10(a).) *Wosniak* actually holds that a court retains subject matter jurisdiction even if the City's argument on mootness were correct. (*Id.*, at 1040; *see also Conservatorship of O'Connor* (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1076, 1087 [distinguishing "subject matter jurisdiction" from when a court may act in excess of jurisdiction].)

"Mootness" cannot deprive this Court of its authority to adjudicate this case. While the Community explains below why the City's mootness argument is incorrect, the Court should deny the challenge to this Court's subject matter jurisdiction on the threshold issue that the City has not stated any basis that would strip this Court's power over this dispute.

#### 3. This Court can render effective relief by invalidating Ordinance 681 because it is the only ordinance controlling the zoning of the Clipper Lot.

The word "supersede" concedes the truth. As defined in the Ninth Edition of Black's Law Dictionary "supersede" means "to annul, make void, or repeal by taking the place of." (See K.C. Mehr Decl., Ex. 1 ["the 1996 statute supersedes the 1989 act"]). Courts agree. (*E.g.*, *Garber*, *supra*,

#### 4. Amendments repeal previous ones.

"Where a section is amended by two different acts, passed at different times, the last amendment repeals the first and operates as the only amendment to the original." (*Garber, supra*, 141 Cal.App.4th Supp. at p, 7; *see also id.* at p. 6 ["When a statute is amended by two different acts, the one passed later in time is the operative law."]; *see also Coca Cola Bottling Co. v. Feliciano* (1939) 32 Cal.App.2d 351, 354; *Sacramento Newspaper Guild v. Sacramento County Bd. of Suprs.* (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 41, 54 ["When a later statute supersedes or substantially modifies an earlier law but without expressly referring to it, the earlier law is repealed or partially repealed by implication."] [citing cases and a treatise].)

The issue of whether a subsequent amendment repeals a previous one typically arises when they are inconsistent, and a court must decide if an amendment's inconsistency with the previous statute indicates an intent to repeal it. By passing identical amendments, that first step of the analysis is unnecessary: The same law on the same issue repeals all previous ones.

On its face, Ordinance 681 amends the Municipal Code to alter the zoning of the Clipper Lot. (City's RJN, Ex. 3 at p, 298 ["Title 17 (Zoning) of the Rancho Palos Verdes Municipal Code is hereby amended..."].) The City admits this basic fact. (Demurrer, at p. 11:5-12:2 [all three ordinances "amended the City's Zoning Code ... including changing the Clipper Lot's zoning;" "[Ordinance 681] contained the same amendments to the City's Zoning Ordinance and Map" at the

urgency ordinances]".) By amending the zoning code regarding the Clipper Lot, Ordinance 681 "repealed" the previous urgency ordinances and "operates as the **only amendment** to the original," i.e., the original designation of the Clipper Lot. (*Garber, supra*, 141 Cal.App.4th Supp. at p. 7 [emphasis added].)

The previous ordinances were not merely superseded; they were repealed. Identical consecutive ordinances cannot logically co-exist because that would create an unworkable legal regime where multiple versions of the same law purport to simultaneously govern the same subject matter, making it impossible for citizens to know which version controls and defeating the fundamental purpose of legal amendments, which is to replace rather than supplement existing law. How would a community member know which amendment controls? How could the designation of the Clipper Lot be clearly identified? Replacing one law with an identically worded and codified law and expecting the two to co-exist creates an absurdity.

#### 7. Urgency zoning ordinances are intended to be temporary measures, which the City confirmed by passing the amendment to the zoning of the Clipper Lot through regular ordinance procedure.

California allows cities to pass ordinances through "regular" or "urgency" procedures. (Gov't Code, sections 36930-36934 [regular ordinances] 36937 [urgency ordinances].) When a city can make appropriate urgency findings, a city may adopt an ordinance on an urgency basis which allows the ordinance to take immediate effect, rather than the usual thirty days after a second reading and passage. (*Id.*, 36937, 65858(a).)

The State established a specific urgency procedure that cities must follow to adopt ordinances "prohibiting any uses that may be in conflict with a contemplated general plan,". (*Id.*, section 65858.) The city must provide findings "that there is a current and immediate threat to the public health, safety" that justifies the immediate action. (Gov. The City passed its urgency amendments in accordance with section 36937 procedure, despite providing purported "Urgency Findings" under Government Code section 65580, the statute which addresses the affordable housing crisis. (Demurrer, at p. 9, n. 1 [citing Exs. 1 & 2]; Ex. 2 at pp. 151-154.) By using section 36937 urgency statute instead of section 65858 one, the City attempted to avoid the 45-limit on the ordinance.

But the temporary nature of section 65858's limit on the effectiveness of urgency ordinances illuminates the nature of urgency ordinances with regard to zoning. They are not intended to be used in place of regularly passed ordinances. Instead, they are intended to be temporary measures. Affected citizens lose the thirty-day window to stop the implementation of the ordinance only when a city can properly articulate the need for "immediate preservation of the public peace, health or safety." When a municipality decides, as the City did here, to amend the same zoning code with a

On the first issue, "development" under the Coastal Act is given "an expansive interpretation." (*Pacific Palisades Bowl Mobile Estates, LLC v. City of Los Angeles* (2012) 55 Cal.4th 783, 796.)

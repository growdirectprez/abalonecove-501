## MEMORANDUM OF POINTS AND AUTHORITIES

### I. INTRODUCTION

In its Petition, Petitioner challenges the City's June 18, 2024 approval of Ordinance No. 681 (the "Rezoning"), which among other things rezoned the Clipper Lot from the RS-4 zone to the RM-22 zone. (Petition, paragraph 52.) Petitioner alleges that (1) the Rezoning is inconsistent with various City adopted land use plans, including the City's General Plan (Petition, paragraph 1, 126-134); (2) the City violated the Coastal Act and the certified Land Use Plan of its Local Coastal Specific Plan (the "LCP") by approving the Rezoning (Petition paragraphs 135-152); (3) the Rezoning is void because then Mayor Cruikshank had a financial conflict of interest, pursuant to the Political Reform Act, that a allegedly prohibited him from participating in the decision to approve the Rezoning (Petition, paragraphs 9, 102-125, 153-163); and (4) then Mayor Cruikshank had a common law conflict of interest that precluded his participation in the City Council's decision to approve the Rezoning (Petition, paragraphs 164-172).

Respondents strongly dispute each of the material allegations in the Petition and contend the Petition substantively lacks any legal merit. However, for purposes of this Demurrer, Respondents contend that Petitioner's challenge to the Rezoning is moot because prior to the City's approval of the Rezoning on June 18, 2024, the City Council approved two urgency ordinances that rezoned the Clipper Lot from the RS-4 Zone to the RM-22 Zone. (*See* Urgency Ordinance Nos. 678U and 680U, Respondents' Request for Judicial Notice ("RRJN"), Exhibits "1" and "2.") The Petition does not challenge the City Council's adoption of these urgency ordinances and the time to challenge them has long since expired. Even if this Court were to ultimately determine the City's approval of the Rezoning was invalid for any of the reasons set forth in the Petition and ordered the City to rescind its approval of the Rezoning, the Clipper Lot would remain zoned RM-22 based upon the City's previous adoption of Urgency Ordinance Nos. 678U and 680U (separately "Ordinance No. 678U" and "Ordinance No. 680U").^1 The City adopted Ordinance Nos. 678U and 680U in order to comply

### III. STANDARD OF REVIEW

A complaint must contain "a statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language." (Code Civ. Proc. section 425.10.) A plaintiff must plead such facts as are necessary "to acquaint a defendant with the nature, source and extent of his claims." (*Doe v. City of Los Angeles* (2007) 42 Cal. 4th 531, 550.)

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.30 and 430.50, a party objecting to a complaint may demur to the entire complaint, or any count alleged within, where defects appear on the face of the complaint. Further, Code of Civil Procedure section 1089 allows for the filing of a demurrer, verified answer, or both in response to a petition for writ of mandate. The grounds upon which a defendant may demur are found in Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, which provides in pertinent part:

> The party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may object, by demurrer or answer as provided in section 430.30, to the pleading on any one or more of the following grounds:

> (a) The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.

> (e) The pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.50(a) provides that a demurrer to a complaint may be taken to the whole pleading or to any causes of action stated therein. In determining the sufficiency of a complaint against a demurrer, a court must "accept as true all material allegations of the

(*Crosstalk Productions, Inc. v. Jacobsen* (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 631, 635.)

### IV. REGULATORY BACKGROUND FOR THE ADOPTION OF A HOUSING ELEMENT

Every City and County in the state must adopt a Housing Element that complies with Article 10.6 "Housing Elements" of the Government Code, commencing at Government Code, section 65580. Housing Elements are adopted every eight (8) years, with each eight year period referred to as a cycle. (Gov. Code, section 65588(e)(3)A.) The most recent cycle for the City was the Sixth Cycle. The City's statutory deadline for adopting the Sixth Revision to its Housing Element was October 15, 2022. (*See* Senate Bill, No. 197, approved June 30, 2022.)

### V. REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS FOR REZONING PROPERTY LOCATED WITHIN A COASTAL ZONE

The Coastal Act "defined the Coastal Commission's mission to protect the coast and to maximize public access to it." (*Keen v. City of Manhattan Beach* (*Keen*) (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 142, 145, review denied (June 29, 2022) [citing PRC, sections 30001.5, 30330].) The Coastal Act's primary means accomplishing its mission is the local coastal program ("LCP") that must be adopted by each local government on the coast, such as the City of Rancho Palos Verdes.

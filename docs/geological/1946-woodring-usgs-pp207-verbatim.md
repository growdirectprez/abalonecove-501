## ABSTRACT (p. 1)

The abstract establishes that the Monterey shale has an exposed thickness of about 2,000 feet and is divided into three mapped local members -- the Altamira shale (thickest), the Valmonte diatomite, and the Malaga mudstone. The Altamira includes two named tuffs: the Portuguese tuff bed and the Miraleste tuff bed.

Key structural statement from abstract: The structure is a general anticline with a deep-seated fault along the north border. The strongest deformation occurred during late Pliocene, with less marked deformation during the middle Pleistocene and weaker deformation near the end of the upper Pleistocene.

The abstract notes that thirteen main marine terraces of upper Pleistocene age are recognized, most clearly defined on the windward west slope and the lower part of the leeward east slope.

---

## GEOGRAPHY -- SURFACE FEATURES (p. 8)

The authors describe the Palos Verdes Hills as a miniature Coast Range area of low altitude, with a maximum northwest-southeast length of about 9.5 miles and a width of 4 to 5 miles. San Pedro Hill, the highest point, has an altitude of 1,480 feet above sea level.

Critical passage on the south coast topography: The crest and greater part of the upper slopes form a rolling upland characterized by smoothly rounded hills and wide, gently sloping valleys. The lower slopes are marked by a series of coastal terraces. Deep canyons advancing inland across the terraces are destroying the rolling upland.

On the south coast specifically: The west and south coasts are bordered by a sea cliff that has in general a height of 100 to 150 feet. At Long Point, on the south coast, the height of the cliff is exceptionally low, 50 feet, whereas at Bluff Cove and Malaga Cove, on the west coast, it is exceptionally high, 300 and 200 feet respectively.

---

## GEOGRAPHY -- LAND USE (pp. 8-10)

The document records the state of development at the time of the field work and publication. The Palos Verdes land grant, comprising the western two-thirds of the hills, was being held for residential and estate development. The text states: "Most of the development so far is in the Malaga Cove, Valmonte, Margate, and Miraleste districts, which are under the control of the Palos Verdes Estates, and in the Rolling Hills district."

This is significant: at the time of writing (field work 1930-35, published 1946), the Portuguese Bend and Abalone Cove areas were undeveloped. The south coast was grazing land and undeveloped hillside.

## MONTEREY SHALE -- GENERAL RELATIONS (p. 13)

Strata of Miocene age rest directly on the Franciscan schist basement. The Miocene deposits overlap the schist northward, with the strata overlying the schist on the north slope of the hills being younger than those on the south slope of the central part of the hills, where the base of the Miocene section is not exposed.

The Miocene deposits include hard silica-cemented shale and soft shale containing siliceous microfossils as the most conspicuous constituents.

The Monterey shale of the Palos Verdes Hills is of late middle to upper Miocene age, with a maximum exposed thickness of about 2,000 feet.

### Critical passage on bentonitic tuff as lubricant (p. 15):

The document describes two named tuffs of stratigraphic importance: the Portuguese tuff, a thick bentonitic tuff at the top of the lower Altamira, and the Miraleste tuff, a thin pumiceous tuff in the middle of the Altamira. These bentonitic tuffs are critical because they serve as slide planes -- a fact documented explicitly on page 18 and again on page 117.

### Rock type definitions (p. 14):

The authors define the key terms used throughout:
- Chert: Hard massive dense vitreous rock composed principally of silica
- Cherty shale: Hard platy dense vitreous rock composed principally of silica. The most common type in the Palos Verdes Hills
- Porcelaneous mudstone: Hard massive nonlustrous rock composed of silica and fine-grained clastics
- Diatomite: Soft massive or laminated rock containing abundant diatoms

The text also notes that volcanic material occurs generally as distinct beds in the Altamira shale, consisting of more or less bentonitic tuff and pumiceous tuff. The Portuguese tuff is at the top of the lower Altamira; the Miraleste tuff is in the middle.

## ALTAMIRA SHALE MEMBER (p. 16)

The oldest member of the Monterey is designated the Altamira shale member. The type region is on the south slope of the hills adjoining Altamira Canyon (see Plate 4). The Altamira is thicker than the overlying members and covers a much larger area. In the type region it has an exposed thickness of about 1,000 feet. On the north slope the thickness decreases to about 600 feet, owing to overlap on the schist basement.

The Altamira is divided into lower, middle, and upper parts characterized, respectively, by the prevalence of silty and sandy shale, cherty shale, and phosphatic shale. Each part includes a breccia composed of schist debris in the lower part, sandstone and conglomerate in the middle part, and thick beds of coarse-grained blue-schist sandstone.

## LOWER PART, INCLUDING PORTUGUESE TUFF BED (pp. 16-18)

### Portuguese Canyon Area Stratigraphy (p. 17)

The lower part of the Altamira shale crops out in the area adjoining the extensive landslide inland from Portuguese Point and Inspiration Point. The best exposures were found in Portuguese Canyon and the unnamed canyon between Portuguese and Klondike canyons. Silty shale and sandy shale are the prevailing rocks in that area. Porcelaneous silty shale, porcelaneous mudstone and siltstone, altered tuffaceous material, limestone, and sandstone are the principal minor constituents.

The measured section in Portuguese Canyon documents 277 feet of lower Altamira strata (combined thickness from two measured sections, pp. 17-18).

### Portuguese Tuff Bed (pp. 17-18):

The top of the lower part of the Altamira is defined by the Portuguese tuff bed, a thick bed of light-colored bentonitic tuff. In the type region it has a thickness of 55 feet. At the landward end of Inspiration Point the tuff is abruptly upturned on the south limb of an anticline (see Plate 5) and is about 60 feet thick.

Critical passage on the tuff as slide plane: The text documents that on the west side of Abalone Cove the tuffaceous material is exposed through a thickness of 30 feet, and the debris indicates that it continues upward through an additional thickness of about 30 feet. The scattered outcrops of the Portuguese tuff that were recognized are shown on the geologic map (Plate 1). Tuff debris, doubtless derived from this bed, was found at many localities along the north margin of the extensive landslide in the Portuguese Canyon district and in stream cuts within the landslide area. The authors state: "Inasmuch as the tuff, dip toward the landslide along its east, north, and northwest margins, this bentonitic tuff probably acted as a lubricant for a mass of rocks that slid down dip into a structural basin and moving seaward overrode the lower marine terraces."

This is the single most important passage in PP207 for the 0 Clipper Road argument. Federal scientists explicitly identified the bentonitic tuff as a lubricant for landslide movement, and documented the landslide as a pre-existing feature -- years before Tract 14649 was recorded in 1949.

### Bluff Cove Area (p. 18)

Strata exposed in the sea cliff at Bluff Cove, at the west end of the eastward-plunging Bluff Cove anticline, appear to represent the lower part of the Altamira shale but are lithologically different from those already described. The Portuguese tuff was not recognized in this region.

A sketch of the sea cliff at Bluff Cove is shown in Figure 5. The stratigraphic relations of some of the lithologic units are uncertain, owing to extensive slides and talus and to incomplete knowledge of the structural features. The schist-debris breccia forming the high greenish cliff appears to represent the oldest strata. Bedding is not clearly discernible in the breccia, but there is a vague suggestion of anticlinal arching toward the top of the cliff. The breccia consists of a rude mass of schist slabs embedded in an unsorted greenish muddy matrix.

## MIDDLE PART, INCLUDING MIRALESTE TUFF BED (pp. 19-23)

### Bluff Cove-Malaga Cove Area (pp. 20-21)

The conglomerate and sandstone exposed near the top of the cliff at Bluff Cove are assigned arbitrarily to the middle part of the Altamira. The conglomerate consists principally of schist pebbles, and some beds contain angular pieces of schist in addition to pebbles.

The text describes that strata north of the slide, immediately adjacent, are folded in a narrow isoclinal anticline (Figure 6). The authors note: "The strong deformation appears to have been localized by bentonitic tuff, which may have acted as a lubricant." This is the second explicit reference to bentonite as a slide-plane lubricant.

The text also notes that slide debris conceals the south boundary of the breccia, that it is inferred that the talus conceals a fault, and that the exposures are complicated by extensive slides and talus.

### Altamira Canyon-Portuguese Canyon Area (pp. 21-22)

The best section of the middle part of the Altamira shale is found along Altamira Canyon and its tributaries, where the structure is simple and the rocks are generally well exposed. A measured section in the east fork of Altamira Canyon totals 670 feet thick.

The measured section includes many thin beds of bentonitic tuff. The mottled tuff in unit 62 was selected as the probable equivalent of the Miraleste tuff.

### Lunada Bay Area (p. 21)

A virtually continuous section complicated by minor folds extends from a horizon apparently low in the middle part of the Altamira shale upward to the Valmonte diatomite in the sea cliff between Bluff Cove and Lunada Bay.

## UPPER PART OF ALTAMIRA (pp. 28-31)

### Malaga Cove Area (pp. 28-29)

Strata assigned to the upper part of the Altamira shale are exposed in the sea cliff southwest of the Malaga Cove beach clubhouse, near the mouth of Malaga Canyon. They consist of phosphatic shale with which cherty shale and limestone are interbedded. The proportion of cherty shale and limestone decreases upward in the section. The section measured in the sea cliff southwest of the mouth of Malaga Canyon totals 272 feet, 2 inches.

The section is continued southwestward along the sea cliff, with an additional 139 feet, 2 inches measured. The authors note evidence of faulting at the locality where the sections join.

### Lunada Bay Area (pp. 29-30)

Phosphatic shale is exposed in road cuts on the flanks of the syncline on the upper slope of the hills in the southeastern part of the Malaga Cove residential district inland from Bluff Cove.

Bituminous and phosphatic shale crop out along Lunada Bay on the south limb of the syncline emerging on the coast on the south side of Palos Verdes Point Lunada Bay. The phosphatic layers are grayish-brown on unweathered surfaces and greenish yellow on weathered surfaces. The section measured in Agua Amarga Canyon at the head of Lunada Bay documents upper and middle parts of the Altamira totaling 135 feet, 2 inches.

### Crest of Hills (pp. 30-31)

Phosphatic shale, including toward the top thin laminae of diatomaceous shale and diatomite, underlies the flanks of the broad, shallow syncline along the crest of the hills, but is well exposed only in the cliffs near the head of Altamira Canyon and its tributaries and in highway cuts. A section measured in a tributary of the middle fork of Altamira Canyon totals 265 feet, 2 inches.

## STRUCTURE -- GENERAL FEATURES (p. 110)

In general the greater part of the Palos Verdes Hills is characterized by relatively simple structure, the major folds being broad and gentle, but in certain areas -- the entire north border, the area between western San Pedro and Palos Verdes Drive East, and a small area on the south coast at Whites Point and nearby -- the structure is complex, the folds being narrow, steep, and locally overturned.

---

## STRUCTURE -- BLUFF COVE AND NEARBY AREAS (p. 111)

At Bluff Cove and nearby the structural features have a southwestward trend, swinging more toward the west. The northeastward-plunging Bluff Cove anticline and the basin-shaped syncline farther inland, in which the Valmonte diatomite member of the Monterey shale is mapped, are examples. The syncline just mentioned is part of a general synclinal area trending almost at right angles to the trend of the hills and bending westward near the coast. Faults are recognized or inferred to be present in the sea cliff in the Bluff Cove area.

The minor isoclinal anticline shown in Figure 6 was detected inland. The localization of movement along a bentonitic tuff, which may have acted as a lubricant, is noted.

---

## STRUCTURE -- REMAINDER OF HILLS (p. 112)

In the remaining parts of the hills the structure is relatively simple, the main folds being broad and gentle. Their prevailing trend is northwest, but in the western part this trend is more westward and the folds merge into the southwestward-trending folds of the Bluff Cove and adjoining areas. At many places there are sharp minor folds of local extent, and locally, as at Bluff Cove (Figure 5), sharp minor folds are overturned. An example of local differential deformation is shown on Plate 7, A. In the area shown diatomite and diatomaceous shale are more strongly deformed than underlying cherty shale and limestone.

## PHYSIOGRAPHY -- MARINE TERRACES (pp. 113-115)

Marine terraces are the most striking physiographic feature in the Palos Verdes Hills. An old surface of considerable relief preserved as a rolling upland along the crest and upper slopes of the hills is thought to have been formed before the submergence and emergence that produced the terraces.

Thirteen main marine terraces of upper Pleistocene age are recognized. The terraces are designated by number in ascending altitudinal order. The first terrace is the last to be formed. The 13 main terraces range in altitude from approximately 100 to 1,300 feet.

On the leeward (east and northeast) slopes, in the Portuguese Bend area, the continuity of terraces is broken by the steeply eroded slope and by landslides. On the leeward east slope in San Pedro the fourth and fifth terraces are also wide.

---

## PHYSIOGRAPHY -- ROLLING UPLAND (p. 116)

The crest and upper slopes of the hills are characterized by wide swalelike valleys of gentle gradient that stand in marked contrast to the narrow steep-gradient canyons at lower levels. The wide valleys and intervening low smoothly rounded divides form a rolling upland. Plate 4 shows the upland skyline and the deep canyons trenching the upland surface in the area inland from Inspiration Point.

Figure 16 shows stream profiles with hanging valleys. Profile B is labeled "Altamira Canyon, south slope of hills" and clearly labels a "Landslide area" on the south coast between the canyon mouth and the Pacific Ocean. This is direct cartographic evidence that the USGS mapped the landslide area on the south coast in the 1930s.

The text discusses how the rolling upland contains several undrained depressions, which are most numerous at the northwest end of the hills. Several explanations are considered, including that they result from underground solution of thin beds of limestone and settling along the gentle-gradient valleys of the rolling upland, and that decrease of the drainage areas resulted from the tapping of the drainage basins of the upland by steep-gradient canyons.

## MINOR PHYSIOGRAPHIC FEATURES -- LANDSLIDES (p. 117)

This is the critical section. The complete description reads:

"Landslides. -- An extensive landslide area forms the hummocky topography inland from Portuguese Point and Inspiration Point (pl. 5). As explained on page 18, the landslide is attributed to movement into a structural basin along a gliding plane formed by water-soaked bentonitic tuff. Smaller landslides found elsewhere on steep slopes are not known to be related to particular rock types."

"Landsliding or slumping of a different type took place a quarter of a mile east of Point Fermin in 1929. A semielliptical area extending 1,000 feet along the 100-foot sea cliff and 400 feet inland moved seaward as a body, leaving a main fissure 5 to 10 feet wide and an irregularly fissured zone as much as 50 to 100 feet wide. The sliding was attributed by Miller to seaward down-dip movement on slippery shale on the south limb of the Point Fermin anticline. Soon after it was formed, the fissure was filled with fossiliferous San Pedro sand from Second and Beacon Streets. Movement that took place again in 1940 suggests that the slumped mass rotated upward as it moved toward the ocean and that stability has not yet been reached. As a result of exceptionally heavy rains the Point Fermin slide was active again in March 1941."

This passage establishes that federal scientists documented two distinct phenomena prior to 1946:
1. The extensive Portuguese Bend landslide complex -- attributed to water-soaked bentonitic tuff as a gliding plane
2. The Point Fermin landslide of 1929, with recurrent movement in 1940 and 1941

The Miller reference is: Miller, W. J., The landslide at Point Fermin, Calif.: Sci. Monthly, vol. 32, pp. 464-469, 6 figs., 1931.

## EVENTS SINCE EMERGENCE OF LOWEST TERRACE (p. 117)

Along the north border of the hills the lowest terrace was deformed following emergence and deposition of the nonmarine cover. On most of the west coast and along virtually the entire south coast the lowest terrace has been destroyed by cutting of the present off-shore platform.

## MINERAL RESOURCES -- OIL POSSIBILITIES (pp. 118-119)

Asphalt is abundant locally in the Altamira member of the Monterey shale and occurs at places in the Valmonte diatomite and Malaga mudstone members. Bedding and joint planes in blue-schist sandstone in the upper part of the Altamira on the east side of Point Fermin contain much asphalt and some layers of sandstone are impregnated with it. Fractures and joints in sandy limestone at the junction of the main branches of Agua Amarga Canyon contain asphalt. Beds of vitric volcanic ash exposed in Peck Park in strata assigned to the Altamira and similar material in the Valmonte along the coast between Cabrillo Beach and Fort McArthur Lower Reservation are stained black with asphalt.

The table of wells drilled for oil in Palos Verdes Hills and near north border of hills (pp. 118-119) lists 18 wells, none on the south coast.

## KEY FINDINGS FOR THE 0 CLIPPER ROAD ARTICLE

### 1. Federal Documentation of Pre-Existing Landslide (1946, based on 1930s field work)

The USGS documented the Portuguese Bend landslide complex as a pre-existing geological feature in 1946, based on field work conducted in 1930, 1933, and 1935. This is 13 years before Tract 14649 was recorded (1949) and 10 years before the catastrophic 1956 slide. The landslide was not a surprise event -- it was a known geological condition mapped by federal scientists.

### 2. Bentonitic Tuff Identified as Slide Plane Lubricant

The authors explicitly identified water-soaked bentonitic tuff (the Portuguese tuff bed) as the gliding plane for the landslide. Key language from p. 18: the tuff "probably acted as a lubricant for a mass of rocks that slid down dip into a structural basin and moving seaward overrode the lower marine terraces." This mechanism -- water-saturated bentonite creating a slide plane -- was understood by federal geologists decades before residential development began.

### 3. Photographic Evidence Labels the Landslide

Three separate illustrations explicitly label the south coast landslide area:
- Plate 4: "Foreground and middle distance are part of landslide"
- Plate 5: "Irregular topography at right is part of landslide"
- Figure 16B: Stream profile labels "Landslide area" on the south coast

### 4. Bluff Cove Area -- Slides and Structural Complexity

The sea cliff at Bluff Cove is described as having "uncertain" stratigraphic relations "owing to extensive slides and talus and to incomplete knowledge of the structural features" (p. 18). The area was already characterized by active slope failure when USGS geologists examined it.

### 5. Bentonite as Lubricant -- Documented Twice

The role of bentonite as a slide-plane lubricant is documented in two separate passages:
- Page 18 (Portuguese tuff bed as gliding plane for the extensive landslide)
- Pages 20-21 (bentonitic tuff acting as lubricant in Bluff Cove area deformation)

### 6. Tuff Debris Found Throughout Landslide Zone

Tuff debris from the Portuguese tuff bed was found "at many localities along the north margin of the extensive landslide in the Portuguese Canyon district and in stream cuts within the landslide area" (p. 18). The debris distribution confirms the landslide was extensive and active enough to break up and redistribute the tuff bed across the slide mass.

### 7. South Coast Terrace Destruction by Erosion and Slides

The authors note that on the leeward east slope, in the Portuguese Bend area specifically, "the continuity of terraces is broken by the steeply eroded slope and by landslides" (p. 113). The south coast landscape was being actively modified by slide processes at the time of mapping.

### 8. Point Fermin Slide -- Recurrent Instability Pattern

The 1929 Point Fermin slide, with documented recurrence in 1940 and 1941, demonstrates a pattern of chronic, water-activated slope instability on the south coast. The mechanism was seaward down-dip movement on slippery shale -- the same general mechanism as the Portuguese Bend complex. Federal scientists noted that "stability has not yet been reached" as of 1946.

### 9. Altamira Shale -- Inherently Problematic Geology

The entire Altamira shale member, which underlies the south coast from Portuguese Point to Bluff Cove, is described as consisting of silty shale, sandy shale, bentonitic tuff, and porcelaneous rocks -- a lithologic assemblage that, when saturated, creates ideal conditions for slope failure. The 1,000-foot thickness of this material dipping seaward on the south slope of the hills is the fundamental geological hazard.

The question is whether the developers of Tract 14649 and Tract 23434 knew or should have known what federal scientists had already published.

## THE PALOS VERDES PENINSULA: ITS PHYSIOGRAPHY, LAND USE AND GEOLOGIC SETTING

Perry L. Ehlig
Department of Geology
California State University, Los Angeles
Los Angeles, CA 90032

### INTRODUCTION

The Palos Verdes Peninsula, as it is most frequently called by its residents, forms a northwesterly dipping dome-shaped ridge, 9 miles long by 5 miles wide, which bounds the southwest margin of the Los Angeles Coastal Plain. The peninsula is bordered by the Pacific Ocean on the south and west, the Los Angeles and Long Beach Harbors on the east and the greater Los Angeles metropolitan area on the north. Because of its elevated position (its crest rises to an altitude of 1480 feet at San Pedro Hill), spectacular views are available from almost every location within the peninsula. The views in combination with a mild, nearly smog-free marine climate and an attractive urban setting make buildable land of the peninsula among the most valuable in southern California. Most of the area has been developed under strict controls designed to minimize grading and preserve the area's natural environment. But the area is not without problems.

Landslides occur in many places, the largest and most destructive of which are on the south flank of the peninsula where wave erosion has removed support from seaward dipping bentonite layers within the Miocene bedrock. A few landslides are of historic origin, such as the Point Fermin landslide at the southeast corner of the peninsula. Most are of pre-historic origin but several of these have reactivated within historic time and some are presently active. In the case of the 270 acre Portuguese Bend landslide, it has been continuously active for the past 25 years with average displacements of about 10 feet per year within its central part. As one would anticipate, the landslides have had a significant economic impact on the area. In addition to direct damage from active landslides, there is loss of income from land which cannot be developed because it is within potentially unstable bedrock areas or areas of ancient landslide of questionable stability. Also there is usually a reduction of property values in stable areas surrounding landslides because of the nearness or association of the area with landsliding.

The purpose of this guidebook and field trip is to acquaint you with the nature and geologic setting of the Palos Verdes landslides, the methods used to investigate them, and the efforts undertaken, or proposed, to control them. Those participating in the field trip should soon appreciate the challenges and difficulties of practicing engineering geology in the Palos Verdes Hills. When you look at the landslide on the east side of Point Fermin during our first stop, you will marvel at the audacity or ignorance of man for building at the top of a vertical sea cliff, 100 feet high, with beds dipping out of the cliff in an unsupported manner. Conversely, however, when you view the west side of Point Fermin you will marvel at nature's ability to resist sliding. For instance, wave erosion on the west side has almost cut away re-

sistant sandstone capping seaward dipping shale beds at the base of the point. Geomorphically, the South Shores landslide should be obvious and its elite mobile home park may appear to be a reasonable way to use a landslide, but you will enter the Klondike Canyon landslide without realizing it. In fact, geologists failed to recognize this ancient incipient landslide until it moved a small amount in 1980. Our stop in this area will focus on the problem of determining the bedrock structure beneath thick terrace deposits. Everybody will recognize the Portuguese Bend landslide as we begin our tour of its ever changing landscape, yet nobody can satisfactorily explain the mechanics of its movement. Stability calculations indicate the shearing strength along its base is significantly less than can be obtained from laboratory tests. Without a sound understanding of its mechanics we cannot be sure what is needed to stop it. Some believe the Portuguese Bend landslide should be left to run its course but most of those who observe the plume of muddy ocean water drifting southward from its toe or drive the disrupted highway across it or see the devastation within it are in favor of stabilizing it. A number of geologists have been intrigued by this slide and some early attempts were made to arrest its movement without success. From the Portuguese Bend landslide we will proceed to the adjoining Abalone Cove landslide which has been stabilized, or nearly stabilized, by a dewatering system which has removed 75 million gallons of water from it during the past two years and continues to remove water at a nearly constant rate of about 100,000 gallons per day. Finally, the trip will conclude at the crest of the hills near the Flying Triangle landslide. This ancient landslide demonstrates the need for better understanding of landslide mechanics. Calculations using shearing strengths from laboratory tests demonstrated the stability of the landslide was adequate for residential development but nature expressed a contrary opinion when a segment of the slide began moving in 1980 affecting four existing homes and one under construction. This slide is still active and the subject of a multi-million dollar lawsuit.

### PHYSIOGRAPHY

The physiographic evolution of the peninsula began during the Late Pliocene when the sea floor was uplifted by the growth of a doubly plunging anticline on the southwest side of the Palos Verdes fault within the nearshore reach of the California Continental Borderland. The peninsula emerged as an island during the Early Pleistocene through the combined effects of local and regional uplift. Continued uplift coupled with sedimentation within the Los Angeles Basin caused the peninsula to become part of the mainland during the Late Pleistocene. At present, the peninsula forms a coastal projection which protects the southwest part of the Los Angeles Coastal Plain from marine erosion. Were it not for this protection, the Los Angeles

coastline would likely trend in a nearly straight line from the Santa Monica Mountains near Pacific Palisades to the San Joaquin Hills near Huntington Beach and would encompass at least 300 fewer square miles of land than at present. Thus, the Palos Verdes Peninsula plays a vital role in the physiographic evolution of southern California.

The development of landforms within the peninsula has been controlled by a combination of marine and nonmarine processes. As the peninsula rose from the sea during the Pleistocene, a flight of wave-cut benches was eroded into its flanks as a result of glacial eustatic changes in sea level (see Bryant, this vol.). The combined effects of stream erosion and slope processes have created an area of gentle rolling hills above an altitude of about 1000 feet. Between about 200 and 1000 feet above sea level, streams have eroded steep-sided canyons into the marine terraces. Below 200 feet the marine terraces are almost continuously preserved beneath a cover of nonmarine deposits except where affected by landslides. Holocene wave erosion has created a cliff, generally 100 to 150 feet high, along the ocean-facing south and west flanks of the peninsula. The north and east flanks have gently sloping margins in most places.

### LAND USE

During the early part of this century the Palos Verdes Peninsula was used primarily for agriculture, particularly dry farming in gently sloping areas. Farming is still undertaken locally, but urbanization has taken over most areas suited for development. Prior to World War II, dense urbanization was restricted to the San Pedro area overlooking Los Angeles Harbor on the east side of the peninsula. Some development took place on the north and northwest sides but was mostly of low density. Following World War II development took place throughout the peninsula and has continued to present. Most of the remaining undeveloped land either has stability problems or is publicly owned.

Four cities have their boundaries entirely within the peninsula. These include Palos Verdes Estates which occupies the northwestern part of the peninsula, Rancho Palos Verdes which includes most of the southern part and segments of the western and eastern parts, Rolling Hills which incorporates most of the upland topography near the crest of the peninsula, and Rolling Hills Estates which occupies much of the north flank. San Pedro on the east side of the peninsula is a part of the City of Los Angeles. Small areas within the northern and eastern parts are unincorporated and are serviced by the County of Los Angeles.

Much of the hilly terrain has low density development with many lots an acre or more in size. This includes all of Rolling Hills and parts of the other three local cities. These areas were developed with as little grading as possible in order to preserve the natural topography. Most of these areas have on site sewage disposal. Landslides and related slope stability problems have occurred in a number of these areas. Many residents realize stability problems could be reduced by installing sewer systems but they are reluctant to install sewers because of the cost. It would also be desirable to perform extensive regrading in some areas to solve stability problems but there is general opposition from those who wish to maintain the natural appearance of the area. Thus, geologists working in the area soon learn that their solutions to geologic problems must also be scientifically sound,

### GEOLOGIC SETTING

The marine Middle Miocene to Early Pliocene Monterey Formation constitutes the exposed bedrock over most of the Palos Verdes Peninsula and is the host for all major landslides. It rests unconformably upon the Mesozoic Catalina Schist. The marine Pliocene Repetto Formation is locally exposed along the northern margin of the peninsula. Early Pleistocene marine Lomita Marl, Timms Point Silt and San Pedro Sand are present along the northern and eastern margins. Pleistocene marine terrace deposits capped by nonmarine deposits rest on wave-cut benches which were the flanks of the peninsula as it rose from the sea. Dune sand which drifted eastward from the beach to the north of the peninsula has encroached upon the northern margin. For detailed descriptions of these formations and the stratigraphic nomenclature, paleontology and geologic structure of the Palos Verdes Peninsula, the reader should refer to Woodring, Bramlette and Kew (1946). This guidebook includes an article on the chronostratigraphy of the Monterey Formation by Chandler Rowell and an article on the marine terraces by Mark Bryant.

The Mesozoic Catalina Schist is of minor significance to the engineering geology of the area but played an important role in the development of the Monterey Formation. The schist contains high pressure-low temperature mineral assemblages believed to have formed within a subduction zone during the Late Cretaceous (Platt, 1976). About 16 m.y. ago at the beginning of Middle Miocene, the schist suddenly appeared at the surface in the southern California Continental Borderland where it formed a largely submerged basin and range topography. The method of its arrival at the surface is a matter of speculation. My preferred hypothesis involves eduction of the schist from beneath the continental margin off Baja California as visualized by Dixon and Farrar (1980), followed by northward transport by strike slip faulting essentially as outlined by Crouch (1979). According to this hypothesis the basin and range topography was formed tectonically by extension resulting from divergent motion along the boundary between the Pacific and North American plates. The Monterey Formation of the Palos Verdes Peninsula was deposited in an isolated offshore basin within this terrain. The basin was isolated from significant sources of continentally derived sediment.

The Monterey Formation is divided into three members each of which might appropriately be granted formational status. These are the Altamira Shale, Valmonte Diatomite and Malaga Mudstone (listed from oldest to youngest). The Altamira Shale crops out over most of the peninsula and has an exposed thickness of about 1000 feet with another 1000 feet concealed beneath the western part of the peninsula. Beds onlap in an eastward direction across initial topography on top of the Catalina Schist such that the Altamira Shale has a total thickness of only 500 feet beneath the northeastern margin of the peninsula. All but the upper most 150 feet of the Altamira Shale was deposited synchronous with local submarine volcanism and contains sills and irregularly-shaped intrusions of basalt and minor pillow basalt. Because this stratigraphic sequence contains abundant tuff, tuffaceous mudstone and tuffaceous shale, I refer to it as the tuffaceous member of the Altamira Shale. It also contains thin bedded porcelanite and cherty shale derived from diatomaceous sediments and beds of impure limestone and dolostone and some sandstone. In the area

around Portuguese Bend, the Portuguese Tuff forms a distinctive unit, as much as 75 feet thick, within the lower-central part of the Altamira Shale. It probably represents rapid deposition subsequent to a submarine volcanic eruption during a single eruptive cycle. Another distinctive but thinner tuff, referred to as the Miraleste Tuff, occurs in the upper part of the Altamira Shale. Most tuff has been converted to bentonite and it is the low shearing strength of the bentonite that is responsible for most landslides in the area.

The upper 150 feet of the Altamira Shale was deposited after local volcanism terminated; consequently it contains no intrusions and little volcaniclastic sediment. The lower 50 feet of this section consists primarily of shert and porcelanite derived from diatomaceous sediment with a few limestone interbeds. It is referred to me as the cherty member and can be recognized in the presence of sod if concealed several inches in diameter, prevalent in chest near the center of the member. The cherty member is overlain by diatomaceous, phosphatic, diatomaceous shale with interbeds of limestone and siltstone. This is referred to as the phosphatic member by me. At the east end of the peninsula, particularly near Point Fermin, the cherty and phosphatic members and the lower part of the overlying Valmonte Diatomite interfinqer with lithic sandstone derived from a Catalina Schist source located to the east of the hills.

The Valmonte Diatomite crops out over an area of nearly a square mile along the crest of the peninsula and is a nearly continuous belt along the northern and eastern margins. The basal part is locally present along the western margin of the peninsula. It is about 500 feet thick and derived mainly from biogenic material, particularly diatoms. Phosphatic nodules, siliceous limestone beds and concretions, thin beds of sandstone and a few beds of vitric ash are minor lithologies. The Valmonte Diatomite was deposited over a period of about 5 m.y. (Rowell, this vol.) and is more easily faulted than source of sandstone.

The Malaga Mudstone and overlying Repetto Siltstone are restricted to the northern and eastern margins of the peninsula and are of minor importance from the standpoint of engineering geology. Both formations were deposited in deep water and contain elastic and clay derived from a continental source.

Starting in Late Pliocene and continuing to present, the Palos Verdes Peninsula has been arched upward as a broad doubly plunging anticline along the south side of the northeasterly-trending Palos Verdes fault. In places the limbs northerly dip beds are tilted 30 or more degrees. Smaller folds are superimposed upon the main fold. Their trend is northeast to west-southwest to the main anticline and have gently dipping axial planes. Several tight upright folds are present along the northern margin close to the Palos Verdes fault. North to northeast-trending folds are locally present in the Altamira Shale. Some may have formed during emplacement of volcanic intrusions. The Altamira Shale locally contains overthrusts to recumbent folds involving strata a few tens of feet thick. These appear to have formed by gravitational sliding prior to lithification of the strata.

Small landslides occur throughout the peninsula but large landslides are restricted to the south flank where wave erosion has removed support from seaward dipping bentonite beds (Fig. 7). The Point Fermin landslide which initially moved in 1929 and reactiva-

ted in 1940 has its base along a clay layer only a fraction of an inch thick near the top of the Altamira Shale. Its instability is easily explained in terms of ordinary landslide mechanics. The other large landslides have their bases along bentonitic layers within the tuffaceous member of the Altamira Shale. They possess a variety of unusual features which are addressed by articles in this guidebook. Monte Ray discusses the South Shores landslide and the remedial grading undertaken to permit development of an elite mobile home park within it. Dating by the carbon-14 method indicates it moved about 35,000 years ago. The modern sea cliff has eroded across the lower part of the slide exposing Altamira Shale which appears to be undisturbed except at the base where marine shale was thrust over Pleistocene terrace deposits. Samples were used to define the structure of the slide but its base could not be identified beneath much of the area. The Klondike Canyon landslide, described by Scott Kerwin, was identified in 1980 because a small amount of movement processed along also in head. The position of the toe is undetermined but generally assumed to be along the beach. I suspect the toe is deformed by folding but others may disagree. There is an apparent groundwater role, perhaps the surfacing of an artesian well which burst forth at about 150 gallons per minute from a reference well near the beach. The Klondike Canyon landslide is part of the ancient Portuguese Bend landslide complex which covers an area of about two square miles and produces an amphitheatre shaped embayment along the south flank of the peninsula. Karl Vonder Linden and Eric Lindvall discuss the Portuguese Bend Landslide of Los Angeles County. It is infamous because the County of Los Angeles was found legally responsible for its reactivation in 1956 and damages of about ten million dollars, and because its movement has continued during the ensuing quarter century. The mechanics of its movement are enigmatic because analyses that have been done to obtain strengths along its base tell us less than can be obtained from laboratory tests. Perry Ehlig of the Abalone Cove landslide with settlement data obtained from the Abalone Cove landslide and Kathleen Ehlig and Bob Bean discuss Abalone Cove dewatering operations. Dewatering costs are paid for by taxing the owners of property. Gary Lass highlights what it was like to make possible to create the Malone Cove Landslide of an ancient slide, the first of its kind in the State of California. Gary Lass and Jack Eagen describe the structure of the ancient landslide complex updune from the Malone Cove landslide and the stabilization methods under consideration at this time. Mark Bryant discusses the geology of the Livingston Quarry area adjacent to the landslide complex and the measures designed to avoid adverse effects on its stability during and following quarry development.

The authors of this guidebook hope their contributions will help develop your understanding of the geology of the Palos Verdes Peninsula and that much of the material presented will be applicable to geologic investigations elsewhere, particularly those involving landslides.

## CHRONOSTRATIGRAPHY OF THE MONTEREY FORMATION OF THE PALOS VERDES HILLS

H. Chandler Rowell
Exxon Co., USA
440 Benmar
Houston, TX 77001

### ABSTRACT

The chronostratigraphy of the Monterey Formation in the Palos Verdes Hills is established on the basis of a diatom zonation and radiometric dates. The Monterey Formation was deposited over a time span of about 12 million years (m.y.) extending from about 15.5 m.y. ago to about 3.5 m.y. ago. The Altamira Shale member of the Monterey was deposited within a period of about 3 m.y., from 15.5 to 12.5 m.y. ago. It ranges from 1,000 to 2,000 feet thick and contains the bentonitic strata responsible for large-scale landsliding and the basalt intrusions and extrusions which form erosionally resistant sea cliffs. The Valmonte Diatomite member of the Monterey spans a period of 5.5 m.y. from about 12.5 to 7 m.y. ago. Its thickness is about 500 feet. The Malaga Mudstone member of the Monterey covers a time span of about 3.5 m.y. from about 7 to 3.5 m.y. ago. Its thickness is probably on the order of 500 feet, but there are no exposures of a complete section within the hills.

### INTRODUCTION

The marine Monterey Formation constitutes the near-surface bedrock over most of the Palos Verdes Hills. It unconformably overlies Mesozoic Catalina Schist, which is locally exposed in the northcentral part of the hills, and is overlain by Pliocene Repetto Siltstone and Pleistocene sediments along the northern and eastern margins of the hills. Terrace deposits cap benches cut into the Monterey Formation by wave erosion as the hills rose above sea level during the Pleistocene. As mapped, described and dated by Woodring et al. (1946), the Monterey Formation consists of three members: Altamira Shale of Middle to early Late Miocene age, Valmonte Diatomite of middle Late Miocene age and Malaga Mudstone of late Late Miocene age. Subsequent advances in radiometric dating, magnetostratigraphy and biostratigraphy have made it possible to establish the more detailed and precise chronostratigraphy presented here.

Woodring et al. (1946) and Rowell (1981) have used benthic foraminiferal and diatom biostratigraphic zonations respectively to date the Palos Verdes Monterey Formation. Kleinpell's (1938) Relizian Stage corresponds to the lower Altamira Shale, the Luisian Stage to the middle Altamira Shale and the Mohnian Stage to the upper Altamira Shale and the Valmonte Diatomite, and the Delmontian Stage to the Malaga Mudstone. Barron's (1981) diatom zonation can be used only in the upper Altamira Shale, Valmonte Diatomite and Malaga Mudstone where the siliceous skeletons have not been recrystallized into porcelanite and chert.

### STRATIGRAPHY OF THE MONTEREY FORMATION

During deposition of the Monterey sediments the Palos Verdes area was part of the greater Los Angeles Basin. Uplift of the hills into an anticlinorium structure occurred along the trend of the Palos Verdes Fault (Figure 1) during the late Pliocene and Pleistocene (Nardin and Henyey, 1978). Associated processes of folding, faulting and mass sediment movement (slumps) deformed the stratigraphic sequence (Rowell, 1981).

Woodring et al. (1946) presented a composite stratigraphic column and designated three members of the Monterey: the Altamira Shale, the Valmonte Diatomite and the Malaga Mudstone (Figure 2). They further divided the Altamira Shale into lower, middle and upper parts. Ehlig (per commun., 1982) prefers to divide the Altamira Shale into three lithologically dis-

tinct members: a tuffaceous member including the Portuguese and Miraleste Tuffs, a cherty member, and a phosphatic member. The latter two form the upper part of the Altamira Shale of Woodring et al. (1946).

At approximately 6 MYBP a widespread hiatus is reflected in North Pacific ocean sediments suggesting a major shift in ocean current patterns (Barron, 1980). This corresponds to the onset of the Malaga Mudstone. The Malaga Mudstone in turn shows similarities to the lower part of the Capistrano Formation (Rowell, 1981), although the two are not entirely time equivalent. It may be hypothesized that the same tectonic events which induced the Malaga/Capistrano terrigenous input into the California Borderland also induced changes on a circum-Pacific scale.

### CONCLUSIONS

Using the absolute time scale correlated to the diatom zonation of Barron (1981) the following conclusions can be drawn concerning the chronostratigraphy of the Monterey Formation in the Palos Verdes Hills.

1. The Monterey Formation in the Palos Verdes Hills ranges from about 15.5 MYBP to about 3.5 MYBP.

2. The Tuffaceous member of the Altamira Shale ranges from 15.5 MYBP to 14.5 MYBP.

3. The cherty member is of short duration between 14.5 MYBP and 14.2 MYBP.

4. The Phosphatic member ranges from around 14.2 MYBP to 12.5 MYBP.

5. The Valmonte Diatomite may extend from 12.5 MYBP to as high as 6.9 MYBP.

6. The Malaga Mudstone ranges from around 6.9 MYBP to 3.5 MYBP.

Correlation of the Palos Verdes Hills stratigraphic section to other areas of the Los Angeles Basin suggests that the Malaga Mudstone may be facies related to the Capistrano Formation. Both may have been deposited in response to major tectonic events of circum-Pacific extent.

## GEOMORPHOLOGY, NEOTECTONICS, AND AGES OF MARINE TERRACES, PALOS VERDES PENINSULA

Mark E. Bryant
Converse Consultants, Inc.
1440 South State College Boulevard
P.O. Box 4206
Anaheim, California 92803

### ABSTRACT

The 13 marine terraces of the Palos Verdes Peninsula comprise one of the most complete sequences of emergent marine terraces in the southern California coastal area. Previous studies of the Palos Verdes terraces have generally indicated that they are all of Upper Pleistocene age. However, current data evaluated in this study suggests a Lower Pleistocene age for the high terraces, with the highest (13th) terrace being an estimated 1.4 million years old. An average tectonic uplift rate of 0.3m/1000 yr has been determined based on recent amino-acid dating of fossil bivalves, while somewhat uplift rates up to 1.15 m/1000 yr are inferred on the basis of terrace correlation to reported paleo-sea level high stands, additionally. Additional amino-acid dating of multiple terraces should provide valuable information as to the absolute ages of the Palos Verdes terraces, aid in deciphering the apparent complex neotectonics of the peninsula, and possibly contribute to resolving the Pleistocene sea-level record.

### INTRODUCTION

The staircase marine terraces of the Palos Verdes Peninsula are among the most prominent geomorphic features of the southern California coastal zone. These emergent Pleistocene marine terraces are the product of repetitious, long-term tectonic uplift of the peninsula, and subsequent tectonic uplift. A Lower Pleistocene age has been hypothesized for the older and higher marine terraces based on one date, including amino-acid dates and Pleistocene sea level determinations. If more of the recent shorelines can be accurately dated, then neotectonic reconstruction of the peninsula would be possible. In addition, dated marine terraces could provide supplemental information pertaining to marine hypotheses and sea-level chronology during the Pleistocene.

### PREVIOUS STUDIES

Early pioneers who identified the existence of marine terraces in southern California include Blake (1856) and Johnson (1894). Regional studies of marine terraces throughout California were conducted by Lawson (1893), Smith (1933), and Palmer (1967). Specific localities in southern California have been investigated, including descriptions and correlations of marine terraces, such as Ventura (Putnam, 1942), Torrance-Santa Monica area (Poland, 1933), and San Diego (Kern, 1977, Figure 1).

Marine terraces were first recognized on the Palos Verdes Peninsula by Lawson (1893), who identified 11 terraces levels. Woodring and others (1946) defined an estimated 13 marine terraces between sea level and San Pedro Hill at an elevation above 1415 ft, and numbered them from 1 to 13 in ascending order. The marine terrace deposits have been referred to collectively as the Palos Verdes Sand by Malnicowski (1976) and

Kennedy (1975). However, the number sequences and designation of the first or lowest terrace at the Palos Verdes Hills by workers and others (1946) have been adopted here. Four, possibly five, submerged marine terraces were delineated offshore of the Palos Verdes Peninsula by Emery (1958).

Recent attempts to date and correlate marine terraces in the southern California coastal zone have been undertaken by many investigators (Szabo and Rosholt, 1969; Kaufman and others, 1971; Birkeland, 1972; Harmon and others, 1981; Muhs and Szabo, 1982), only a few terraces on the Palos Verdes Peninsula have been dated by the amino-acid or similar quantitative radiometric/geochemical methods, and the results indicate a relatively wide range of ages. Most researchers have considered these terraces to be of Upper Pleistocene age, while others suggested that the highest terraces could be of Lower Pleistocene, or possibly Upper Pliocene age (Szabo and others, 1981; Muhs, Palaeogeography and Biomes, 1983). At present, the exact ages of the marine terraces are uncertain, and the neotectonic history of the peninsula remains unresolved.

### ABSTRACT

Commencing in 1957 subsurface exploration of the South Shores landslide by bulldozer, backhoe, bucket auger, rotary mud and rotary air methods of excavation were utilized to investigate the depth and extent of the landslide for potential development feasibility. Geologic data from regional mapping and exposures in the seacliff and surf area were correlated with deep bore-hole gamma log data to provide structural control for cross sections and stability calculations. Laboratory tests were performed on bulk and undisturbed samples to determine shear strength parameters of angles of internal friction and cohesion for use in stability calculations. Carbon-14 dating indicated the landslide was of ancient origin (±35,000 years b.p.) and engineering analyses indicated it possessed a factor of safety against sliding of about 1.1. Analysis indicated load redistribution during grading could result in a slight increase in factor of safety. This increase in factor of safety coupled with the prevention of water from entering the slide plane led to the conclusion that the stabilization methods would improve conditions relative to stability wherever possible. This necessitated establishing a fairly accurate estimate of the existing factor of safety based on establishing slide zone strength parameters.

### AGE OF THE LANDSLIDE

The age of the landslide was demonstrated by carbon-14 dating of fragments of carbonized wood collected from old alluvium which was deposited when the upper portion of Barrow Canyon was dammed the slide. Previous field work indicated the slide was ancient. In that, slide scarps had been subdued by erosion, depressions had been filled with sediment and colluvium, and older debris had developed a fairly thick soil cover.

In the area where the carbonized wood was collected the older alluvium fans uphill into alluvial deposits of the canyon. This alluvium accumulated when the slide dammed the natural drainage. Upstream from the original slide dam was eventually breached and the now existing Barrow Canyon was incised into the old alluvial. Most of the old alluvium is exposed in the walls and bottom of the canyon north of the landslide area, whereas the sediments and eroded debris on the slide's upper portion eventually grade down to the geologic terrace. Inasmuch as the carbonized wood occurs in the lowest possible portion of the old alluvium, the slide was probably only a few years old at the time the wood was buried and thus the age of the wood gives the approximate age of the young side; date the canyon was dammed by the slide. However, because examination of the old alluvium shows it is in place and reveals no faulting or other deformation indicating renewed landsliding, the age of the wood dates the age of youngest landslide movement as about 35,000 years old. The slide movement probably occurred as a single event.

The field investigation included mapping of existing exposures of bedrock and terrace deposits along the beach and sea cliff face and in the canyon beds intersecting the slide area and sea cliff. ### GRADING AND STABILIZATION MEASURES

In the early 1970's grading commenced on the South Shores Trailer Park site. During the grading all canyons were cleaned out and subdrains were installed. Nearly all of the cut slopes except for a few low height slopes exposing terrace deposit clays were buttressed (with subdrain installed) or received stabilization fills. Exposed level cut areas were capped with concrete soil blankets to inhibit infiltration of surface water. Surface drainage was improved to carry runoff to storm drains. Utility line trenches were inspected, logged and backfilled in proper sections with cohesive soils.

Outlet locations of drainage devices were carefully checked for clearance and free flow. Special recommendations were provided for such diverse items as rock disposal, stockpiling of topsoil, clubhouse, service area and sentry gate foundations, overhanging decks, exclusion of pools, and special landscape watering systems.

Finally, recommendations were made for installation of monitoring wells at three locations: a north well to a depth of 171 feet, a central well to a depth of 174 feet, and a south well to a depth of 271 feet. The wells were installed in 1973 and provisions were made for quarterly monitoring.

### SUMMARY

The work done in the South Shores Trailer Park resulted in a slight improvement in the stability because of load redistribution of mass. Measures taken to impede infiltration of water and to improve drainage made the development possible without compromising the stability. As with many of the landslides in the Palos Verdes area (refer to other articles in this guidebook), water is critical to the stability of the South Shores landslide. The presence of brittle, fractured, siliceous and cherty strata in the South Shores landslide, coupled with removal of the toe by coastal erosion, allows this slide to drain more freely than many of the currently active slides which exhibit impoundment of water in the area. This free draining factor is important to the maintenance of stability of the South Shores landslide. Finally, future development in the area should include measures to impede infiltration, improve drainage and distribute soil loads advantageously in order to avoid buildup of the water table and to maintain or improve the present margin of stability, particularly in upslope areas.

### ABSTRACT

Evidence of recent ground movement in the Seaview Tract in the vicinity of Klondike Canyon adjacent to the active Portuguese Bend Landslide has generated concern for the stability of the area and has precipitated an intensive and cooperative program of investigation by consultants for the City of Rancho Palos Verdes and large private landowners. Extensive subsurface exploration has determined the general geologic structure, stratigraphy and delineation of the boundaries of an apparently reactivated ancient landslide. The depth of the slide and the cause and mechanism of its movement are still items of concern and under study. Methods of dealing with the potential problems, liabilities, and procedures to minimize further movement are addressed.

### INTRODUCTION

The apparent stability of the seaward dipping bedrock exposed in Klondike Canyon has been a question in the minds of many geologists since reactivation of the nearby Portuguese Bend Landslide in 1956. Evidence of recent ground movement in the existing Seaview Tract and on the ridge separating Klondike Canyon from the active slide has precipitated a relatively intense review of investigation to evaluate the gross stability of the area. Robert Stone and Associates (RSA is the principal major landslide investigator in the area) and the City of Rancho Palos Verdes included excavation and logging of 66 borings and 14 trenches, installation of 4 inclinometers to monitor possible slide movement, and the fortuitous establishment of an artesian development well. Much of the information gathered is still being analyzed, but a number of conclusions and recommendations have been made.

### GEOLOGY

In 1946, the USGS published Professional Paper 207, "Geology and Paleontology of Palos Verdes Hills, California" by W. P. Woodring, M. N. Bramlette, and W. S. W. Kew. Field work for the publication was initiated by Kew in 1921 and was substantially supplemented by Woodring and Bramlette during the 1930's. Formation names and stratigraphic nomenclature in general use by geologists presently working in the area are taken from this paper.

### Stratigraphy

Bedrock underlying the Klondike Canyon area primarily consists of siltstone, shale, tuff and minor sandstone of the Altamira Shale member of the Monterey Formation. Also evident in the area are a few small sill-like bodies of basalt (including the "Martinson Sill") which may be directly associated with the intrusion of a relatively large body of basalt exposed in Livingston Quarry (Parcel 7).

Lithofacies in the area are interbedded and interfingered. The basalt occurs in sills which have been injected into unconsolidated or only partially lithified marine sediments. These intrusions may have created local relief of the depositional surface by deforming the overlying sediments.

These injected bodies, localized facies changes, and possibly small scale submarine slumping, may account for the observed interfingering and healing of shale, siltstone and sandstone. Alteration (hydrothermal alteration?) of portions of the sedimentary section adjacent to the intrusions has further complicated local stratigraphy.

In spite of this apparent variability, inspection of a number of bedrock exposures, core samples, bucket auger borings and boring logs in the Klondike Canyon area has shown that the local stratigraphy is generally consistent and a few key horizons can be correlated across the area. Correlations based on geophysical logging (primarily gamma naturally) of some borings and geologic mapping of exposures has significantly simplified bedrock description in the logs by softening the effects of weathering, poor sample recovery (cuttings in rotary borings), slight facies changes, etc.

### Portuguese Tuff

The Portuguese Tuff is the most prominent and distinctive unit in the area, and is often used as a reference point in discussing the stratigraphy. The type section for the Portuguese Tuff, as described by Woodring et al. (1946), is exposed in Klondike Canyon adjacent to Palos Verdes Drive South. Locally, the Portuguese Tuff shows a fairly consistent thickness of 45 to 60 feet. It is composed primarily of dense bentonite and bentonitic tuff, except for an approximately 10-foot thick interval that is highly silicified, which is present near the center of the unit.

### Prominent Marker Units

Three other prominent mappable units having distinctive characteristics are present in the area. The "Sandy Tuff" is exposed in the sea cliff and consists of 10-foot thick bed of weathered tuff. This unit is typically overlain by a discontinuous, irregular bed of well cemented fine silty sandstone; used as a structural relationship, the "Sandy Tuff" occurs approximately 220 feet stratigraphically above the Portuguese Tuff.

Marker Bed No. 1 is a 2.5 to 3.5-foot thick, thinly laminated siliceous siltstone (dolomitic) that is predominately exposed on the sea cliff at three locations in the intertidal zone. Two interbeds of 10 feet; siltstone layers occur approximately between Marker Bed No. 1 and about Marker Bed No. 1 to weathering thicknesses, spacing and resistive differences (hardnesses) make these beds easily identifiable. Marker Bed No. 1 occurs approximately 35 feet stratigraphically below the "Sandy Tuff", and, accordingly, 185 feet above the Portuguese Tuff.

An unnamed interval of altered tuff with a distinctive and thickness similar to the "Sandy Tuff" occurs intermediate to Marker Bed No. 1 and the top of the Portuguese Tuff. This unit was recognized in borings at the top of the sea cliff north of the axis of the "Coastline Monocline-Anticline", and at about 120-150 feet above the Portuguese Tuff.

The "Volcanic" is a 6 to 10-foot thick basalt sill exposed in Klondike Canyon near borings RPV-1, 2, 4, and F-5. The "Volcanic" is located 65-85 feet below Marker Bed No. 1. Inspection of the boring logs shows a relatively constant thickness of about 10 feet and the Volcanic is located within a fairly consistent interval of about 45 feet below the base of the "Sandy Tuff" and the base of the tuff and the base is the "Volcanic." Petrographic thin-sections of core from borings RPV-3 and F-5 were described by Karl Vonder Linden as basalt; but since field descriptions of this unit have referred to it as an altered tuff because of its high degree of weathering. Very similar, distinctive profiles in gamma logs of the borings confirm the continuity of this interval over much of the area.

### Martinson Sill

The major conduit for the artesian flow noted in Borings RPV-1 appears to be a 5 to 15-foot thick granular interval immediately above the "Volcanic." This section assemblage at this sand-like interval from samples in RPV-1 determined that this unit is equivalent to the basalt sill previously described by Karl Vonder Linden. The intrusive lithic sandstone was described by Karl Vonder Linden as basalt.

The apparent close association of granular clastic material with the intervals of tuff and basalt may be related to the environment of deposition and intrusion of these units. A period of volcanic activity and/or, possibly associated local uplift, may have resulted in deposition of granular clastic sediments and/or fracturing of lithified sediments. In the case of the intrusions, it seems likely that fluids and gases associated with an intrusive would have a tendency to follow an unconsolidated or only partially lithified sandstone. The existence of conduits or reservoirs for groundwater in the bedrock may have a relation to the genesis of the landslides.

Unfortunately overlying the bedrock in most of the area are uplifted, largely non-marine terraced deposits. These terrace materials were deposited on a number of wave-cut benches in the bedrock. The lowest part of the terrace section is typically comprised of a relatively thin fossil/ferous marine deposit containing numerous rounded cobbles. The present shoreline and intertidal zone is an analogous surface of marine erosion.

### Portuguese Tuff

The Portuguese Tuff as it occurs in the subsurface is important not only because it is a distinctive mappable unit for structural interpretation, but also because it is directly related to large landslides in the area. This association with large failures is due primarily to the structural attitude (subsequent beds dipping seaward). The relative weakness of the altered tuff beds (bentonite) prevalent in the stratigraphic interval bordering the Tuff is also an important factor. Under conditions of high moisture content and loss of effective shearing (fissural slip or landsliding) the shear strength of these bentonite layers decreases significantly.

Other probable factors in the development of these large landslides are the expansive and essentially impermeable characteristics of the bentonite. Volume changes associated with intermittent wetting and drying of these clay layers could create distinct planes of weakness in the bedrock intervals. The relatively large thickness of the Portuguese Tuff acts as an effective groundwater barrier. In addition to the decrease in shear strengths created by continuous interaction with high moisture contents, below impounded WPF(s) in seaward-dipping strata below the Tuff would create hydrostatic forces which could significantly affect the stability of the overlying bedrock. Perched groundwater in the stratigraphic interval immediately above the Tuff would create similar forces and conditions.

### Structure

The gross structure of the Palos Verdes Peninsula is that of a broadly plunging anticline with an axis near the crest of the hills trending northwesterly (approximately N60W). Fold axes within the Klondike Canyon area appear to show three general trends: N50-70W, N30-50E, and east-west.

1. Folds trending N50-70W parallel the gross anticlinorium structure of the Palos Verdes Hills and tend to be gentle, broad, open-flexures which dictate the regional dip to the south-southwest. Evidence of these flexures is apparent in the landslide-like topography of the Portuguese Bend Landslide mass.

2. Approximately perpendicular to these seaward dipping flexures are a number of troughs and ridge-like folds showing a general seaward plunge. A small body of water near the eastern edge of the active Portuguese Bend landslide known as the "Lake Livingston" generally occupies a topographic low which parallels one of these structural troughs.

3. The most pronounced folding within the area trends approximately east-west. This trend is akin to the gross structure and for this reason, appears to be the least influenced by the general seaward (southeast) dip of the strata on the south side of the Palos Verdes hills. The major folds exposed in the intertidal zone of Parcel 15 show this east-west orientation.

Bedrock structure in the area is a complex combination of these three basic trends. Folds appear to either be accentuated or flattened in those areas where these trends converge.

Orientation of bedrock strata in the Klondike Canyon area, including that underlying the active Portuguese Bend Landslide, generally shows a regional dip to the southwest at low to moderate angles averaging about 15 degrees. There are significant departures from this general attitude, particularly within the Klondike Canyon area.

The "Coastline Monocline" (trending N30-50E) is generally well defined north of Palos Verdes Drive South, and this abrupt change from the regional dip has limited the easterly extent of the active Portuguese Bend Landslide. Bedrock structure between the "Coastline Monocline" and the easterly portion of Parcel 15 generally conforms to the regional dip, with only subtle folding or warping of the bedrock strata.

Three major folds underlie the eastern portion of Parcel 15 and show a marked departure from the regional dip much like that of the "Coastline Monocline." These three, the Coastline Monocline, Seaview Syncline, and Martinson Anticline have essentially parallel fold axes which trend in an east-southwest direction. The beds which form these folds are prominently exposed in the intertidal zone at the base of the sea cliff, particularly south of the jetty. Inland, the folds have been generally confirmed by borings and geologic mapping of exposures east of the Seaview Tract (east of Forrestall Drive and north of Palos Verdes Drive South). The seaward extension of the "Seaview Syncline" is well-defined in a number of outcrops by underwater exposures of Marker Bed No. 1. These exposures suggest an eastward plunge of the fold axis and possibly a closed structural basin offshore.

East of the "Martinson Anticline" two small, well-defined folds are evident in the cliff and intertidal zone. These folds are significant because they illustrate the complexity of structure where general trends of the folding overlap. Both folds trend approximately N30-50E and show a moderate (20-25 degrees) plunge to the southwest between the sea cliff and shoreline. At the shoreline, the axes of these folds sharply flatten or show downward plunge where they cross a broad flexure which approximately parallels the shoreline (N50-70W), 100 to 150 feet seaward from the shoreline, the same folds again plunge to the southwest at moderate angles, illustrating the stair-like, stepped arrangement of much of the strata in the area.

### Landslides

The controlling factor of most of the large scale landsliding within the Portuguese Bend area is the regional seaward dip of the beds. It appears the major sliding has occurred along underlying seaward dipping planes undercut by erosion of the bedrock at the base of wave-cut terraces, including the present sea cliff. Numerous joint or fissure-related features are present in the bedrock of the upper area because most of the surficial material has been altered to bentonite. The bentonitic is characteristically subject to volume change with increased moisture content and typically has low shearing strength, especially in previously sheared material under conditions of high moisture content. Consequently, high moisture content and the presence of bentonite layers in the bedrock are also factors

contributing to the development of landslides in the area.

### Beach Club Landslide

Eleven borings have been drilled within the well defined bowl-shaped feature of the "Beach Club Landslide." Comparison of the shear surfaces and inspection of the boring logs has revealed some interesting correlations.

Correlation of bedrock units above the shear surface in two borings indicates horizontal displacement of perhaps 175 feet. Assuming a planar shear surface with a dip of 13 degrees, this is equivalent to a vertical displacement of about 40 feet. Inspection of aerial photos predating the construction of Palos Verdes Drive South shows a slight topographic high on the slide mass which probably underlies the fill for the road (see photo, Plate III). This small rise was probably the headwall of the actual slide mass, and post-depositional of displacement. This position suggests a horizontal displacement of about 160 feet.

Reconstruction of the topography indicates, conservatively, at least a 40-foot thickness of the slide mass has been eroded away from the headward portion of the slide before placement of the fill. Erosion is even more pronounced near the toe where the shear plane is only 5 to 7 feet below the present ground surface and daylights in the small bluff above the beach at about 45 feet elevation. This high degree of erosion and the well consolidated nature of infill behind the head area probably indicates it is an ancient feature.

The slide is underlain by a relatively flat, seaward dipping syncline or trough in the bedrock ("Beach Club Syncline"). It appears the failure surface is defined by the trough; terrace(?) deposits along at least a portion of both sides of the syncline probably defined the lateral limits of its movement and consequently the landward extent. It is interesting to note that a projection of the upper surface along a bearing in the bedrock "C-dip" into the terrace deposits near the previously mentioned fractures in Admiralty Drive.

A maximum thickness of 13 feet of displaced bedrock was observed in the borings near the axis of the syncline. The interface between the bedrock and the terrace deposits sometimes acts as a conduit for groundwater, especially in those areas where the sandy marine deposits are present. Weathering of the upper portion of the bedrock and possibly the high moisture conditions in this structural trough may have been factors in the failure of this part of the slide in addition to those previously discussed.

### Klondike Canyon Landslide

The existence of the ancient Klondike Canyon landslide was documented by RSA in 1980. This conclusion was based on subsurface investigations — displaced cracks and fissuring in the Seaview Tract and adjacent undeveloped land. The evidence of movement appears to be centered about Klondike Canyon north of Palos Verdes Drive South and is most noticeable near the head of the slide.

## Summary of the Argument

In October 1939, a group of Palos Verdes Estates residents and current/former Palos Verdes Homes Association board members published this 20-page booklet advocating for incorporation of PVE as a "City of the Sixth Class" under California law. The election was scheduled for December 9, 1939.

The central argument: **the private-governance framework established by Declaration 1 (1923) had failed fiscally.** The Palos Verdes Homes Association had allowed ~800 acres of community property — parks, playgrounds, golf course, swimming club, pool, and 4.5 miles of shoreline — to revert to the State of California for seven years of delinquent County taxes totaling approximately $42,000. The estimated value of the forfeited community property was $1,500,000.

The only way to recover the property was to incorporate as a city. The County and State had assured residents that upon incorporation the community property would be deeded back to the new City, free of the $42,000 back-tax debt and free from the ongoing $5,800/year County tax on that property, because municipally owned property is exempt from County taxation.

## PREFACE (verbatim)

> From the beginning of the Palos Verdes Homes Association until about two years ago, a majority of the residents of Palos Verdes knew very little about the administration of local governmental affairs. The cause of this almost total ignorance on the part of so many residents concerning local matters is immaterial at this time. The main point is that our residents have now been awakened to find themselves face to face with a serious local problem, **THE LOSS OF THEIR COMMUNITY PROPERTY.**

> Quite naturally, those who are sincerely interested in the future welfare of our community wish to acquaint themselves with authentic facts relating to our problems in order to help work out the best solution.

> A considerable number of residents, including some past and present members of the board of the Homes Association, recognized certain problems relating to our local government and set about to study thoroughly the entire subject in the hope of finding the best way to preserve and further the ideals of our community. An almost unbelievable amount of effort has been directed toward the accumulation of facts, and many citizens have contributed both time and funds to accomplish the research needed. Realizing that it is impractical for every interested individual to peruse Homes Association accounts and records, visit the County Road Department offices, consult with County Sheriff's representatives, receive advice from the County Supervisors, study the California Statutes, and perform the large number of tasks required to develop reliable information, it is believed desirable to represent the most pertinent facts in printed form.

> It is therefore, for the purpose of supplying the facts required, to answer the many questions raised in the present endeavor to incorporate Palos Verdes Estates into a City of the Sixth Class under California law, that the following booklet is presented.

> Now that the date of the election has been set, December 9, 1939, to determine whether the people wish to incorporate, it is the desire of those who have worked for an election to decide this question by American methods. **We have procured this privilege for our citizens against almost unbelievable obstacles placed in the way by those who wished to settle the question negatively by denying us the right of an election.** Every resident in our community should familiarize himself with the authentic facts and decide this question according to his own conclusions.

## INTRODUCTORY — Why Incorporate?

> THE PROPOSAL to incorporate the community of Palos Verdes Estates into a City of the Sixth Class is not a new scheme which has been devised recently, but is a natural consequence of growth and development in our community as well as in others. The question of incorporation has been thought of at various times during the last eight or ten years, but those who were in the best position to determine community policy felt that the time had not yet arrived for incorporation. **Just to what extent this opinion was influenced by the real estate interests, who had the voting power to dominate the Homes Association, is a question which cannot be answered.**

> It was reasonable in earlier years, when the community was not sufficiently developed for the usual form of city government to be either legal or practical, that the Homes Association "bridge the gap of time from the beginning of the project until there were enough people under State law to organize the ordinary forms of American government," according to the intention set forth in the second paragraph, page six of our present "Protective Restrictions for Palos Verdes Estates." **That gap of time has long since passed, and we are now faced with the ever increasing needs of a growing community.**

> Under the California statute, a minimum of five hundred residents is required for a given area to incorporate as a City of the Sixth Class. The present population of the area proposed for incorporation is rather accurately estimated as being between **eleven and twelve hundred**, with a total assessed valuation of land and improvements of **$2,627,320**, according to Homes Association records. With two years of building construction to be added to the assessed valuation by the time the city functions, the assessed valuation will be $2,827,320.

> It is desirable that we must preserve the community of Palos Verdes Estates intact, and we must keep it as an ideal residential district, upholding restrictions, its recreational facilities and beauty.

> To fix the absolute necessity for a City on one point only disregarding many others for the moment — **it is the only direct way to repossess our eight hundred acres of parks, playgrounds, golf club and golf course, swimming club and pool, as well as shoreline**, (One payment made on ten year plan of redemption), for we do not own them at the present time. **All our community property has reverted to the State on account of seven years of delinquent County taxes amounting to about forty-two thousand dollars.**

### QUESTIONS

1. Can we raise $42,000.00 to pay this debt and repossess our community property?
2. Can we pay $12,000.00 per year in order to repossess them on the ten year plan? (This figure includes the payment on the principal, interest and current year's taxes.)
3. Do we want the State to place them on the auction block which must be done if a single individual requests it?
4. Do we want to lose the privacy, control and regulation of this property by permitting the State to use what remains after the auction for public playgrounds and picnic grounds?

> Your answer in all cases would naturally be in the negative.

### SOLUTION

> **The County and State authorities have assured us if we incorporate as a City, the community property will be deeded back to the City to be used as formerly (park purposes), the highest purpose intended, free from the debt of $42,000.00 in back taxes and free from the $5,800.00 yearly County tax. Municipally owned property is exempt from County and other taxing agencies.**

### VITAL ISSUES

> Every single lot and home owner should consider the following questions seriously. The first one as mentioned above is paramount.

> A. Do you want to repossess and preserve for all time to come your former eight hundred acres of parks, swimming club and pool, golf club and course, and four and one-half miles of shoreline?
> B. Do you wish to lower your taxes?
> C. Do you wish to have a representative form of American government?
> D. Do you want restrictions to be enforced?
> E. Do you want adequate Law and Order, as well as Fire Protection?

## I. PALOS VERDES HOMES ASSOCIATION (Verbatim excerpts)

### Taxes

> The directors of the Homes Association under the Basic Restrictions have the power to fix and establish annually an assessment on each and every lot or parcel of said real property (now on land and improvement) at a rate equal to, but not greater than that established by the Old City of Los Angeles. &hellip; In 1938 our tax or assessment rate was $1.71 per one hundred dollars assessed valuation on land and improvements, formerly based on the assessed valuation of land only.

> **Unlike real taxes, the Homes Association assessment is merely a second lien on the property. It is subordinate to County, District and other taxes and any other lien, mortgage or incumbrance on the property. It is, therefore, forfeited or lost by the Homes Association upon foreclosure by others.**

> No legal procedure has ever established the Homes Association assessment as a lawful tax, for only municipal, county, state and federal bodies can tax. The recent court action, has simply ruled that IF THE TAX WERE LEGAL it should be applied on the improvements, as well as the land (real property). The courts as yet have not had the opportunity to pass upon the legality of the assessment as a tax.

> **The Homes Association has cancelled $35,367.98 taxes in the past year. It has traded $55,000 in delinquent taxes for water service, the most of which was used by the Golf Course. To date there are $27,319.49 of delinquent taxes on the books with questionable possibilities of collection. In the past year $4,500 has been expended on legal action by the Homes Association.**

### Organization

#### 1. Board of Directors

> The Board of Directors of the Homes Association consists of five members elected at the annual meeting held on the second Tuesday in January by lot owners, having one vote per lot — twenty lots, twenty votes. A quorum or 2640 votes are required to elect a new member to the board, each having a term of three years.

> The five directors appoint a President and Vice President from their own number. **If no quorum exists at the annual or special election, or if conditions prevent a quorum, the directors perpetuate themselves or others in office, electing whom they please when vacancies occur. THEREIN LIES A DANGEROUS CONDITION.**

> The terms of Mr. A. P. Allen and Mr. Fred Roessler, resident directors, expire this January. Will the individual and resident property owners be able to muster sufficient proxies to re-elect them or other individuals to take their places? **If they cannot, the control of the Homes Association returns to the real estate interests.**

> There is a provision in the Palos Verdes Basic Restrictions for recalling directors of the Homes Association. (Page 51 — Basic Restrictions.) The Board of Directors or any director may be removed from office by any member of the Homes Association by a petition signed by members who are holders of record title of at least FIFTY PER CENT OF ALL SAID BUILDING SITES. **This would be impossible to accomplish if needed, unless the real estate interests were of the same opinion because of their vote control.**

#### 2. The Art Jury

> The Art Jury shall have full jurisdiction over all lands and property over which the Palos Verdes Homes Association likewise has jurisdiction. &hellip; The President of the Palos Verdes Homes Association becomes the member ex-officio as successor to the ex-officio member named by Commonwealth Trust Company (now extinct) and shall have power to appoint the other six members as set out in the Basic Restrictions. **More Power than any one man should be given.**

> The members of the Art Jury receive pay for time in attendance at meetings and expenses entailed. **The members of the Art Jury received $2000 last year and $3,016.58 the year before.**

> Any change in restrictions, conditions, covenants, planning, subdividing, street and park landscaping of property within the limits of Palos Verdes Estates must have the approval of both the Palos Verdes Art Jury and Palos Verdes Homes Association. **With the Homes Association controlled by adverse interests and the Art Jury appointed by the President of the Homes Association, our restrictions and general set-up could easily be jeopardized.**

#### 3. The Palos Verdes Police Department

> At the present time Palos Verdes Estates has two policemen. They have been deputized by the County, but on account of our community being unincorporated, they do not have authority to enforce local ordinances (in reality there are no local ordinances.) &hellip; The Sheriff's Office for this territory is located at 108th Street and Vermont Avenue, just 17.1 miles from Palos Verdes Estates Plaza.

> The Sheriff's Office has one car, No. 233, assigned to territory bounded by a section north of 108th and Vermont, south to the western boundary of San Pedro, including Miraleste, to the Ocean, thence west and north to Palos Verdes Estates, taking in the whole peninsula and Rolling Hills, thence north to El Porto Beach near El Segundo, and over thirteen miles directly east to 108th Street and Vermont Avenue.

> The one car mentioned above is supposed to patrol the different unincorporated sections within this total area of over 200 square miles.

#### 4. The Palos Verdes Fire Department

> The Palos Verdes Fire Department at present consists of a **1921 antiquated Ford hose wagon, unable to climb our hills or throw a stream to reach the second story of a dwelling.** The personnel is volunteer.

> This service is augmented by the County Forestry Service, which fire station is located at Florence and Compton Avenues — **approximately thirty miles away**. The equipment that serves this territory is also an antiquated Ford which is capable of arriving here in forty-five minutes if it is not on another call.

### Powers

> The powers of the Board of Directors of the Palos Verdes Homes Association are practically unlimited — another dangerous condition. **They can acquire by gift, purchase, or lease, and operate, maintain, convey, SELL, transfer, improve, mortgage ANY AND ALL real or personal property of the Homes Association.** They can employ a manager, secretaries, engineers, auditors, technical consultants or any other employees or assistants if, in their judgment, they are necessary.

### Disadvantages

> 1. To redeem the Palos Verdes community property, it would cost the property owners $42,000 in back taxes and interest, exclusive of penalties, if we are to remain under Homes Association government alone.
> 2. After redemption of the property, it would cost $5,800 a year for present County taxes, more as time goes on.
> 3. The Homes Association tax limit is too high.
> 4. **Shoreline property could be sold or conveyed, as the Homes Association directors were considering a year ago.**
> 5. The Directors of the Homes Association have too much power.
> 6. The present Police and Fire Departments are neither legally constituted, nor adequate.

### Conclusions

> **A. The Palos Verdes Homes Association has lost over $1,500,000 worth of community property to the State on account of non-payment of County taxes.**

> B. The Palos Verdes Homes Association could easily be controlled by outside interests on account of the voting power of lots.

> C. The Palos Verdes Homes Association assessments (taxes) at their best are nothing but second liens — they have been cancelled, traded and forfeited to the extent of approximately $100,000. (From the records of the Homes Association).

> D. The Palos Verdes Police and Fire Departments are inadequate.

> E. To redeem our community property — to secure our government majority representation — to force collection of our taxes proportionately (no exceptions) and to improve our Police and Fire protection, everyone should VOTE FOR A CITY.

## II. THE DISTRICT SYSTEMS (summary)

> The district is a legally constituted body set up by law to provide funds and government for specific purposes, not for all purposes — but just for the one specified.

Districts potentially applicable to PVE:
- Park, Recreational and Parkway District
- Fire Protection District
- Police District
- Garbage Disposal District
- Lighting District

Already applied via county tax bill: School, Library, Sanitation, and County Flood Districts (totaling $2.50/$100 in 1938, $2.72/$100 in 1939).

**Park District:** Max tax $0.50 per $100 assessed valuation; funds restricted to parks/playgrounds only; no provision to lease back to Homes Association.

**Police District:** Act 6016 General Law; max $2,400/year for police protection — "too small to provide twenty-four hour protection."

**Fire District:** Act 2583 General Law; tax rate set by Board of Supervisors with no definite limit. Reference rates cited: Altadena 23½¢/$100; Flintridge 69¢/$100; Puente 79¢/$100; Lawndale 68¢/$100.

## III. THE CITY OF THE SIXTH CLASS (verbatim excerpts)

### Taxes

> The City Council has the "power to levy and collect annually a property tax which SHALL NOT, without the assent of two-thirds of the qualified electors of such city voting at an election to be held for that purpose, EXCEED ONE DOLLAR on each one hundred dollars of assessed valuation." (Paragraph 862.11, Act 5233 General Laws, State of California.)

Comparable 1938 rates for Sixth-Class cities cited:
- Hillsborough: $0.59
- Atherton: $0.59
- Menlo Park: $0.45
- Belmont: $0.70

### City Organization

> The City Council is composed of five members who are elected by the qualified electors of the City at the general municipal election which is held on the second Tuesday in April in each even numbered year. The members of the City Council **RECEIVE NO COMPENSATION** for their services unless the majority of electors vote to so compensate them. From one of their number they choose the Mayor. The Councilmen hold office for four years.

> **RECALL: The State Constitution reserves to the electors of a City the power to remove or recall any elective municipal officer under conditions provided by law at any time.**

### Limitations on City Power

> 1. The City Council **SHALL NOT HAVE power to sell or convey any portion of any water frontage.** (Paragraph 862.2 Act 5233 General Laws, State of California.) The Directors of the Palos Verdes Homes Association have the power to sell or convey anything belonging to the Homes Association. (Paragraph 19, Basic Restrictions Palos Verdes Homes Association.)

> 2. "The City Council shall not incur any indebtedness or liability in any manner or for any purpose exceeding in any year the income and revenue provided for such year without the assent of two-thirds of the qualified electors of said city." **This law would have prevented the $46,157.07 golf club house and furniture expenditures by the Directors of the Palos Verdes Homes Association and would have saved our present predicament.**

### Bonds

> For a City to issue bonds, it requires at least a TWO-THIRDS MAJORITY of all voters, voting at an election held for that purpose.

> The City of Palos Verdes Estates, when incorporated, will **own outright — clear from indebtedness — all the community property** very few communities could dream of having and all any community could wish for, and therefore would not need to borrow money to purchase anything.

### Semi-Private Property

> All portions of City Property which the citizens decide to be of semi-private nature, as golf course and swimming pool, **could be leased back to the Homes Association at a nominal rental for supervision**, for the City is permitted by law to lease while the Park District is not.

### Miraleste

> The citizens of the Miraleste section of Palos Verdes, adjoining San Pedro, realize that all Homes Association community property now belongs to the State on account of delinquent taxes. They have taken steps to repossess the park land and parkways in their section by petitioning the County Board of Supervisors for a Park District to be formed within their boundaries. This is the most logical thing for them to do since they are too small to incorporate and their only problem is to reclaim their park lands and maintain them.

> They have received but little support from Palos Verdes Homes Association taxes and don't feel they should contribute to our problems. We therefore will lose their financial support, thereby reducing the present Homes Association revenue by one-eighth.

### Conclusions

> **A. A City of the Sixth Class can regain our community property from the State. It is worth $1,500,000. It is what makes Palos Verdes the ideal residential community it is today. It could not be purchased for any price in the future.**

> B. A City of the Sixth Class is economical, has reasonable limits of equitable taxation, and has definite limits on sale of Shoreline property and expenditures.

> C. A City of the Sixth Class is a legal entity already established by law and does not have to conduct expensive legal procedure to establish the validity of its actions.

> **D. A City of the Sixth Class is democratic because it is representative of the majority of people and cannot be controlled by minority interests having financial or selfish gains their goal.**

> E. A City of the Sixth Class is more economical.

## KEY FINDINGS FOR THE FOUNDATION RECORD

The 1939 booklet is among the most damaging primary sources for the theory that the private-governance layer (Declaration 1, Palos Verdes Homes Association, Art Jury) was self-sufficient or financially sound. The documented facts include:

1. **The Palos Verdes Homes Association failed to pay property taxes for seven consecutive years** on approximately 800 acres of community property — parks, golf course, swimming club, and 4.5 miles of shoreline.

2. **The State of California took the community property for $42,000 in back taxes.** Value of property forfeited: $1,500,000.

3. **Homes Association assessments were not real taxes.** They were second liens, subordinate to County tax, subordinate to any mortgage or encumbrance, and lost on foreclosure.

4. **$100,000 in accumulated assessments had been cancelled, traded, or forfeited** — in the single year preceding the booklet's publication: $35,367.98 cancelled; $55,000 traded for water service (primarily consumed by the golf course); $27,319.49 still uncollectible.

5. **The Board of Directors had power to sell or convey community property.** Including, per the booklet, active consideration of selling shoreline property.

6. **Art Jury members were paid** — $2,000 in 1938, $3,016.58 in 1937. The Art Jury had approval power over all subdivision, construction, landscaping, and change in restrictions.

7. **The Art Jury was appointed by the Homes Association President** (himself the successor to the Commonwealth Trust Company ex-officio member). The booklet describes this as "More Power than any one man should be given."

8. **Voting control was based on lots, not residents.** Real estate interests holding many lots could dominate HA board elections. The recall threshold of 50% by building site made recall functionally impossible without the real-estate interests' consent.

9. **The police force was two deputized officers with no authority to enforce ordinances** (because there were none to enforce). Sheriff's patrol was one car across 200 square miles.

10. **The fire department was a 1921 Ford hose wagon that could not reach second-story fires.** County Forestry backup was 30 miles away and 45 minutes out.

11. **PVE incorporated on December 9, 1939** not because the private framework had succeeded but because it had failed. Incorporation was a rescue of the community property that Declaration 1's Homes Association could not fund.

### Implications for the 0 Clipper Analysis

The booklet materially strengthens the Foundation's reliance on the public regulatory framework over the private covenant framework. If Declaration 1's Homes Association lost its own community property for tax non-payment, Declaration 100's Community Association for the non-PVE Lot&nbsp;H area never had any fiscal substance at all. The public-governance layer (RPV incorporation 1973, Coastal Specific Plan 1978, 1980 Wong subdivision approval) is what actually preserved land-use character on the coast.

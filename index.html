What's a Quorum?

By an HOA Community Member on the Palos Verdes Peninsula

2026 · 25 min read

What's a Quorum?

It was at the HOA meeting. We were discussing the community path; what had happened with the storms, the issues with neighbors about access, the infighting about what the board had done, the final decision on a lawsuit that went nowhere, the overspending on an attorney who got us nothing. The seemingly repetitive year after year restating of the basics and going through the motions. Eighty-one lots, five streets, dues under twenty dollars a month.

And I asked: what is a quorum anyway? How do we even make a decision around here?

Nobody knew. Some people said it was a majority. Some of the older neighbors who had been around a long time said it was very detailed and complex. It's in the bylaws. Those bylaws, you know, those are important. Those bylaws need to be updated.

So I went to go look at the bylaws. To figure out what a quorum is. And whether this board and this HOA were just a waste of energy in a place where the city seems to think they have it all under control and can do what they want in terms of planning anyway. Why do we need an HOA for tree trimming? For height variances? If it comes down to it, whoever has the most money wins. Whoever is on the city council when it counts. Whoever knows who.

Could I use my technical background and these AI tools I had been using for development to help the community?

And this is what happened.

The Olmsted Thread

In 1988, I was an undergraduate at USC taking a two-unit seminar. The professor was Kenneth Starr. He told us about Palos Verdes Estates. He described how it had been planned by the Olmsted Brothers under a set of recorded declarations that created an Art Jury, mandated Mediterranean architecture, and governed everything from roof tiles to setbacks. Declaration 1. I didn't really even know where Palos Verdes Estates was at the time, even though I was born and raised in Los Angeles.

But the name stuck. Olmsted. Over the years I spent time in New York, San Francisco, Boston. Olmsted cities, all of them. Central Park. The Emerald Necklace. Golden Gate Park's surroundings. The same design philosophy everywhere: planned communities where landscape, architecture, and covenant work together. I understood it intellectually. I didn't know I'd end up living inside one.

I returned to Los Angeles in the early 2000s and moved to Malaga Cove for three years. That's when I started to understand the hill. Not from a textbook but from driving the roads, walking the bluffs, and going in and out of homes with my wife, who is a realtor. Twenty-plus years on the peninsula. Hundreds of houses. You develop a feel for the place that no document can give you.

And then you find the documents, and they confirm what you already sensed: this place was designed. Every curve of every road. Every setback. Every view corridor. It wasn't an accident. Someone planned it, a hundred years ago, and the plan is still legible in the landscape if you know what you're looking at.

I remembered the Malaga Cove Library. We lived across the street. We loved the concerts in the park there, and I took my kids growing up. It's a wonderful place. My wife told me about the records room at the library, and I started searching the online archives for more information about the area. I was actually looking for some old postcards I had seen before to use for the website, and I stumbled upon the landscape plans for Abalone Cove.

I knew about the Olmsted plans. I knew the vision was never built, and I assumed it was in the Portuguese Bend area where the slide is moving today, where homes slid in the fifties. But then I noticed the lot line.

When I started looking at the drawings on the library website, I noticed they were part of a collection from the Library of Congress and the National Archives. They were part of a larger set of detailed site plans and garden drawings that I downloaded. And in those plans, one of the lot lines in the original plan had the same angle as our tract map. The same bearing. I overlaid them and they matched. I could pinpoint the boundary of Tract 14649 on a drawing made twenty-five years before the tract was recorded. The dream and the deed were the same line on the same piece of land. The Olmsted Brothers drew it. The Vanderlips recorded it. And it's still there. Then AI started calling out the handwritten notes on the drawings; annotations about boundary areas, native canyon features, the steepness of the grade. Details that a person scanning a blueprint might miss but that a machine could read and flag.

Vanderlip

Here's something you might not know about Los Angeles. The same family that helped create the Federal Reserve System hired the architects who planned this peninsula. And the legal architecture they built to govern it; the declarations, the Art Jury, the community associations; is still in force. Some of it, anyway. The parts that are convenient.

In 1882, a Superior Court partition awarded Jotham Bixby a piece of the old Rancho de los Palos Verdes; a Mexican land grant that covered most of the peninsula south of Redondo Beach. Bixby and his syndicate held it for thirty years. Then, in 1913, Frank Vanderlip bought it all.

Vanderlip is one of those figures who shows up in the footnotes of American history more than you'd expect. He was the president of National City Bank, the largest bank in the country at the time. In November 1910, he was one of six men who traveled under assumed names to Jekyll Island, Georgia, where they spent nine days drafting what became the Federal Reserve Act. He knew how systems worked. He knew how to build things that lasted.

What he built on the peninsula was ambitious. He hired the Olmsted Brothers, the successors to Frederick Law Olmsted, the man who designed Central Park, to plan the entire hill. Not a subdivision. A community. Frederick Law Olmsted Jr. moved to the peninsula and built a house there to oversee the work.

In 1925, the Vanderlip syndicate reorganized as Palos Verdes Corporation, a Delaware entity. Over the next several years, PV Corp recorded a series of declarations that divided the peninsula into governed districts, each with its own architectural controls, its own set of rules about what could and couldn't be built.

Declaration No. 1 established Palos Verdes Estates and laid down the template for the Red Tile districts controlled by the Art Jury in PVE and Miraleste. It is the famous document that defines the City Beautiful movement on the peninsula and is the basis for master-planned communities as we know them today. It also famously rolled racial exclusions into the whole planning apparatus and has been the subject of controversy over the years.

There are other documents. Declaration 150 defined the Rolling Hills Community Association, which would later in the 1960s become the city of Rolling Hills. They also created two documents which I couldn't find at the library or online anywhere, for that matter. Declaration 100, which defined a body described as the Palos Verdes Community Association and a subcommittee called the Art Jury. And Declaration 101, where they defined the portions of Lot H where these restrictions apply.

The Olmsted plan was the dream. The declarations were the legal machinery that was supposed to make it real. But the plan was an artist's rendering of the contracts; a vision for how one of the richest men in the world at the time wanted his own front driveway landscaped. It's a driveway plan so grand that when the house wasn't built, the road that was put in its place became a county public works project in 1935, producing a road survey that turned out to be the key to the whole mystery.

The Lawyer Brothers and the Depression

There is a gap in the record between 1930 and 1948 that I haven't fully filled. What I can tell you is what the documents show, and what they suggest.

In October 1929, the same month the stock market crashed, PV Corp recorded Declaration 100, establishing an Art Jury and a red tile district for the Portuguese Bend area. The same system that Declaration 1 created for Palos Verdes Estates. The same architectural controls, the same Mediterranean requirements, the same governance structure. Portuguese Bend was supposed to be another Malaga Cove. Two weeks later, Declaration 101 applied those standards to seven specific parcels covering roughly eleven hundred acres.

In August 1930, PV Corp conveyed three of those parcels to the Filiorum Corporation for ten dollars, nominal consideration, a transfer within the family's network of legal vehicles. The Neptune Fountain was built. La Venta Inn was operating. The Abalone Shore Club had just been incorporated as a private club for the first time.

By 1935, the County of Los Angeles Public Works Department had resurveyed the land through Portuguese Bend for the new proposed route of Palos Verdes Drive South, right through the middle of the estate grounds. The Villa Narcissa that remains today in the Portuguese Bend area is commonly thought of as the Vanderlip mansion. It isn't. It's the cottage. It's barely visible in the Olmsted drawings, dwarfed by the vision of a Versailles-level estate that was never built. This was Frank Vanderlip. He wasn't rich to begin with; he became rich on Wall Street. He saw the homes the Robber Barons of the Gilded Age had built in the Hudson Valley, and he planned to build his own overlooking the Port of Los Angeles and the Catalina Channel. Just months before the Panama Canal opened. Sixteen thousand acres for a hundred and forty-eight dollars an acre. Best land deal of all time.

Then Frank Vanderlip Sr. died in 1937. The sons weren't quite old enough to take over. The Depression was still grinding. The war was coming. For roughly eighteen years, from the crash to the end of the war, PV Corp held the peninsula through what must have been extraordinary economic pressure, maintaining a real estate operation of this scale with no buyers and no income. Through the early fifties the tracts sat unbuilt, partially subdivided. PV Corp sought tax relief. They suspended assessments on HOAs unbuilt and lots unsold to lessen the burden. Any real estate developer knows about carrying costs and the uncertainty of the deal until it's done. They even did reversions to acreage, removing subdivisions and obscuring the tract and parcel lineage.

The men who built the infrastructure of the peninsula were Jay and Donald Lawyer — and yes, that is their actual surname. The Lawyer Brothers were the peninsula's first builders and real estate agents. They built the Neptune Fountain. They built La Venta Inn. They structured the corporate formations, the declarations, the Filiorum transfers. Their work still governs this land nearly a century later.

But their documents are in the evidence room, and the legal architecture they built is remarkably sophisticated.

I'm surprised that Declarations 100 and 101 never came up during the discussions about the beach park in the 1970s. I don't see any mention of them in the articles about the Rolling Hills cityhood battles in the 1960s, when all those legal fights over declaration enforcement were being fought. I only found these documents because AI found the original map references on county engineering diagrams from the 1930s, surveys of Palos Verdes Drive South, one of the most surveyed roads in the world. Nobody may have looked at them before because they never mattered. The land was always private. The declarations were always academic.

But when the Filiorum Corporation dissolved and the family's land speculation finally ended, the restrictions didn't dissolve with it. Recorded declarations run with the land.

The Parceling of the Hill

Before the subdivisions came the private land company. The Vanderlips created the Filiorum Corporation as the family's private holding vehicle for the coastal parcels, governed by Declaration 150 and the same system as Declarations 1 and 100.

Rolling Hills brought its own famous battle. When the newly formed city began to annex properties in the 1960s that were not part of the land area described in Declaration 150, the question of whether these private restrictions could be enforced became a live legal fight. At the time, it was convenient for the city to enforce those restrictions on private property owners. They imposed land use restrictions, view ordinances, and tree ordinances onto private owners; the same kinds of restrictions they will not enforce today.

But before all of that, in the months just after the stock market crashed at the end of 1929, the Lawyer Brothers also parceled and conveyed to the Filiorum Corporation, for the sum of ten dollars, almost twelve hundred acres of land stretching from Abalone Cove to where the Trump National Golf Course sits today; basically the whole stretch of road that the city has closed to cyclists right now because they can't guarantee safety. The grant deed is in the evidence room. Ten dollars for twelve hundred acres of California coastline. It was a transfer within the family's network of legal vehicles, not a sale.

Then came the Depression. Then the war. And then, when times were looking better, they began to subdivide in earnest.

In 1949, PV Corp created three tracts, three sister subdivisions, each with its own HOA and architectural review committee, all created by the same corporation, all subject to the same framework of single-family home restrictions, lot setbacks, and architectural controls that traditional HOAs have today. The West Portuguese Bend Community Association, established by Declaration No. One of Protective Restrictions in 1949. The Portuguese Bend HOA. And the Portuguese Bend Beach Club. Kelvin C. Vanderlip, as president of PV Corp, signed every document. It is interesting to note that the WPBCA's Declaration No. One uses the same legal language and structure as the earlier PVE Declaration 1; the same hierarchy of restrictions, the same enforcement framework, clearly drawn from the same template.

The Declaration of Easements, recorded six days after the WPBCA's Declaration No. One, preserved two paths to the ocean; a bridle trail and a walkway running from Lot 1 to the Pacific. This is one of the few remaining private ocean access easements on the California coast. It exists because someone wrote it into a deed in 1949.

Then, separately, on January 25, 1950, they recorded the Lot H Declaration, reaffirming on the remaining family holdings what they had already declared in 1929. This was the negative space declaration, blanket coverage of the entire remaining privately held parcels. Everything PV Corp still owned in Portuguese Bend that wasn't already in a named tract. Eleven tracts were excepted because they had their own declarations. Everything else was Lot H.

I initially thought this was the key to everything; a blanket restriction that would cover the vacant lot at 0 Clipper. I was wrong. When I read the actual document carefully, the only enforceable restriction it contained was a racial occupancy covenant, now void under California law. The Lot H Declaration has no architectural controls, no building-type limits, no committee authority. It is a negative-space declaration with nothing left in it; except to say whites only. The protection I thought existed doesn't. That was a hard lesson in reading the primary source, not just the summary.

And then the land began to move.

The land speculation became less speculative. The parcels where the grand estate and the private city were going to be built, the red tile district that never was, the Art Jury that never convened, the Mediterranean vision that Declaration 100 had described in such careful detail, began to slide. The dream that the Olmsted map had rendered so beautifully was over. The bentonite had the final word.

When PV Corp sold its stock to Great Lakes Carbon Corporation in 1953 for roughly nine million dollars, the Vanderlip family retained five hundred acres in Portuguese Bend. The corporation was dissolved in Delaware on September 27, 1954. Permanently. The three-year revival window expired in 1957. But the declarations run with the land. They don't need a corporation to enforce them.

The Slide

Underneath the peninsula, about two thousand feet of ancient seabed tilts gently toward the ocean. The geologists call it the Monterey Formation; three layers of compressed marine sediment from the Miocene era. The bottom layer, the Altamira Shale, contains a fifty-to-seventy-five-foot band of bentonite clay called the Portuguese Tuff. When it gets wet, it turns into something close to grease.

In 1956, a county road project cut into the hillside above Portuguese Bend. Water found the bentonite. Two hundred and sixty acres began to move, a reactivation of an ancient complex that covered nine hundred acres of the south-central coastline.

The Ancient Portuguese Bend Landslide had been dormant for roughly a hundred thousand years. Once reactivated, it never stopped. The active slide zone affected approximately a hundred and sixty homes, destroying a hundred and thirty-four of them. It displaced Palos Verdes Drive South. It eliminated the planned extension of Crenshaw Boulevard. Twenty-three steel-reinforced concrete caissons failed to stop it. Earth redistribution, dewatering wells, gabions, drainage improvements, fissure sealing. All failed. The land moves at roughly three feet per year. It has been moving continuously since 1956.

In 1974, a second slide activated in the Abalone Cove area, eighty-five acres, part of the same ancient complex. The city adopted a Landslide Moratorium Ordinance in 1978, covering twelve hundred acres. No new construction on undeveloped land within the moratorium area. The ordinance still stands.

The city's own geotechnical consultants identified what keeps the slide moving. Early residential development used septic systems, leach lines, and cesspools that pumped water directly into the landslide mass. The consultants' phrase was careful: residential plumbing systems were "likely catalysts" for the inception of movement. Groundwater, they concluded, was the controlling factor, and the only factor reasonably manipulable to minimize slide movement.

Lloyd Wright's Wayfarers Chapel, a few miles up the coast, had to be disassembled in 2024. The ground beneath it was moving more than two feet per month. Same formation. Same forces. The chapel's trouble started, as expressed by community members at the planning commission hearing in April 2025, when someone began watering a vineyard on the slope above it.

As of March 2026, GPS monuments in the active zone are recording movement of 2.14 inches per week.

Walk up the creek bed at 0 Clipper Road today and you can pull handfuls of dirt from the ancient landslide deposit with your pinky. It's a canyon. The lot sits at the center of a Venn diagram; landslide moratorium area, flood hazard zone, Coastal Specific Plan Subregion 4, certified coastal zone, high fire severity zone. And the only reason it sits at the center of all of those restrictions is because it is unbuildable. That's not a legal opinion. That's what the ground is telling you.

There is also the matter of the Palos Verdes Blue Butterfly (Glaucopsyche lygdamus palosverdesensis). Listed as endangered by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service on July 2, 1980 under Section 4 of the Endangered Species Act (16 U.S.C. §1533). The parcel is within the range of this species. Any development would need to address the habitat question under Section 7 (16 U.S.C. §1536), which requires federal agencies to ensure their actions do not jeopardize the continued existence of listed species. The butterfly doesn't care about housing elements or planning commissions. It is protected by federal law, and its habitat was here before any of us. The Coastal Specific Plan also identifies the site as a native habitation area. If you download the Indigenous Areas GIS map for the city today, this lot sits in the middle of an ancient village.

Condos vs. the Beach

In 1972, the community that used Abalone Cove faced the same question it faces today.

Karl Rodi, a Beverly Hills attorney, proposed building a hundred and thirty-eight to a hundred and seventy condominiums on Portuguese Point, the headland that separates the cove from the open ocean. Dick Fitzgerald, from the Los Angeles County Department of Beaches, proposed a shoreline park instead.

Dick Karshner, the president of the Abalone Shore Club, organized the vote. The community chose the park.

Abalone Cove Shoreline Park exists today because of that vote. It is one of the few publicly accessible beaches on the Palos Verdes Peninsula. Fifty-four years later, the question is the same: condos or preservation. The parcel is different. The geology hasn't changed.

The Coastal Wall

The Abalone Shore leases that had governed beach access through the Shore Club era were a product of the same Vanderlip framework, private agreements on private land, administered by the family's corporate vehicles. When the Shore Club chose the park over the condos in 1972, it was the last act of that private governance system. What came next was public.

All of this; the declarations, the Art Jury, the covenant structure, the three sister HOAs; happened before the City of Rancho Palos Verdes existed. The city seems largely unaware of its own prehistory.

Rancho Palos Verdes incorporated in 1973, twenty-four years after the Vanderlips recorded their declarations. The reason the city exists is that communities decided to incorporate to stop condo developers. The Marineland debacle. The condo developments being planned in unincorporated county areas. The city was supposed to be the steward of the coastal character. To preserve it. That is literally why RPV was created.

Three years later, the California Coastal Act of 1976 created the permanent California Coastal Commission and required every coastal city to prepare a Local Coastal Program. RPV adopted its Coastal Specific Plan on December 19, 1978, by Resolution 78-61. The Coastal Commission certified it.

The plan divides the coastal zone into eight subregions. Subregion 4 covers Abalone Cove, our community, the vacant lot, the fire station, the park. Policies S4-1 through S4-14 describe in meticulous detail what can and cannot happen here. The plan classifies the area's natural features. It identifies flood and inundation hazards. It establishes coastal resource management zones. The city wrote these policies itself and submitted them for state certification.

Layer after layer of regulation was built to prevent exactly what is being proposed now. The declarations. The city incorporation. The Coastal Act. The Coastal Specific Plan. The landslide moratorium. Each one added by a different authority, in a different decade, for the same reason: this coastline requires protection, not development.

The general plan that RPV adopted after incorporation. The building restrictions the city imposed along the coast. The moratorium ordinance that Chapter 15.20 of the municipal code still enforces today: no new construction on undeveloped land within the twelve-hundred-acre Landslide Moratorium Area absent a Moratorium Exclusion. The ordinance requires landslide abatement measures as conditions of any exception: correction of lot drainage, replacement of septic systems with holding tanks, containment of roof runoff, geotechnical reports, participation in sewer and storm drain assessment districts, hold harmless agreements, and water management systems. The city wrote these rules for this land. They are still the rules.

And then there is the road itself. Palos Verdes Drive South was rerouted after the landslide displaced it. The original Coast Road was renamed. Sea Cove Drive curves through our community along the path the Olmsted Brothers drew in the 1920s, but the road that connects it to the rest of the city has been surveyed, resurveyed, rerouted, and repaired more times than anyone can count. The engineering diagrams from the 1930s, the same ones where AI found the map references to Declaration 100, show a road that was already being rebuilt before the slide even started. Every survey tells the same story: this ground moves.

The Wong Subdivision

In 1980, a man named Wong subdivided the vacant parcel at 0 Clipper Road into five residential lots. Tract 32977. Five lots on a lot and a half acres. That was the density the city considered appropriate for this land, and it is the last subdivision that was actually approved and recorded.

Six years later, for reasons the record doesn't fully explain, the subdivision was reversed. Tract 43725, filed in 1986, reverted the five lots back to a single parcel. Same owner, same engineer. The flood hazard notation carried forward on the new map.

The Wong Family Trust eventually sold to a neighbor in the community. The previous owners, a neighbor in the community, held the parcel for sixteen years. They wanted to do more with it. They explored commercial zoning. They wanted to use it for parking. They proposed a warehouse, a "museum" for their car collection. The city said no. Not enough tax revenue in that. The city held them to what was appropriate for this land.

At the city council hearings, everyone fought and complained. But no one pulled title. No one pointed out the previous subdivision and reversion. It was not easy to find; it was buried in probate and opaque title transfers that were part of an estate being settled. Not that it matters. None of it does, really. But it's the lack of transparency and the attention to detail that can permeate the situation.

In October 2021, the previous owner sold to Clipper Development LLC for $2.2 million. The buyer was Ali Vahdani, a civil engineer from Vernon, California. In November 2025, Hankey Capital issued a $2.1 million commercial loan secured by the parcel.

There is a reason a 1.58-acre lot has sat vacant for a hundred years of California development, owned by the man who invented it. Every owner since has held it, tried to build, and moved on. The pattern is hard to miss. Someone acquires the parcel, holds it, tries to build, and eventually sells to the next buyer. A hundred years of this.

The Rezoning

On April 16, 2024, the Rancho Palos Verdes City Council passed an urgency ordinance rezoning the vacant parcel from RS-4 to RM-22. Single-family residential to multi-family, twenty-two units per acre. They followed it with a second urgency ordinance on June 4 and a regular ordinance on June 18. Three ordinances in sixty-three days.

The proposal called for fourteen townhomes and two cottages, sixteen units on a lot and a half acres. The city calculated density based on the full 1.56-acre parcel. But the assessor's maps show a flood hazard easement that reduces the usable area to roughly one acre. At that size, sixteen units works out to about sixteen per acre, not the ten per acre the city presented.

On June 4, 2024, attorney Kendra Carney Mehr submitted a letter to the city council on behalf of eighty homeowners in Abalone Cove opposing the rezoning. It was thirty-nine pages long. On August 7, an FPPC complaint was filed against the mayor. On September 11, a petition for writ of mandate was filed in Superior Court, Case No. 24TRCP00352. The city filed a demurrer in November. The community filed its opposition in December.

In January 2025, the California Department of Housing and Community Development confirmed that the city could remove Site 16, the 0 Clipper parcel, from its housing element and remain RHNA-compliant. The numbers worked without it. In June 2025, the city council voted to keep it anyway.

In April 2025, the city brought in a consulting firm to present to the planning commission. Their message was reassuring: this was just about numbers. Compliance with the housing element. Nobody would ever actually need permits here. If that is true, if the rezoning is purely theoretical, a paper exercise to satisfy Sacramento, then it raises its own question. Why do it at all? Why rezone a parcel on an active landslide if nobody intends to build? And if someone does intend to build, then the assurance that it's just about numbers was not accurate.

Someone has a two-million-dollar commercial loan secured by this parcel. Someone intends to build.

The housing element exists because Sacramento requires every city in California to plan for its share of new housing. That makes sense in places where housing is needed and infrastructure can support it. The question is whether a vacant parcel on an active landslide, in a flood hazard zone, inside a landslide moratorium area, adjacent to a failing fire station, on a road the city can barely maintain, within a certified Coastal Specific Plan that restricts exactly this kind of development. Whether that is the right place to put sixteen units because a spreadsheet says the numbers need to go somewhere.

The Tools

None of this research would have been possible five years ago, not for one person working alone.

My wife joined the board at the urging of a neighbor. I overheard her preparations for the annual meeting and was dismayed at the lack of participation and the value of the time she and others in the community were spending. The dues were based on 1950s assessments. I began to wonder if there was a better way.

California's Davis-Stirling Act governs how homeowners associations operate; elections, assessments, secret ballots, quorum rules. My wife had volunteered her time to the community, which gave me some insight into HOA governance I hadn't seen before. And I lived in a previous board member's house. That sparked my curiosity.

I thought maybe I could use my technical background to help. Build a community website. Put the important documents online so they were easy to find. Answer the questions that nobody seemed to know offhand. Maybe I could use AI, I had been using it at work, to help build a simple voting engine. Maybe we could actually take a proper vote. Get compliant with Davis-Stirling. Would we use SurveyMonkey? I suggested it at the HOA meeting. Everybody laughed. Some scowled and told me to move on. Said I was interrupting the board. Why was I wasting everyone's time asking questions about procedure? I wasn't laughing. I was just a community member who happened to live in a previous board member's house and got ahold of the records. What's in here anyway? A couple of banker boxes and a mailing tube, sitting in my garage for twenty years. I knew the blueprints were there for the house, and I had thumbed through some of the old files. But I didn't know what I had. I didn't understand why nobody cared about making a real decision, or why certain people wouldn't want us to make one.

When I bought my house, the previous owner had left documents in the garage. A couple of banker boxes and a mailing tube, sitting there for twenty years. I knew the blueprints were there for the house, and I had thumbed through some of the old files. But I didn't know what I had.

Later I learned that the previous owner had served on the HOA board in the 1950s and had been a director of the Abalone Shore Club, a private club that leased property from the Vanderlip family and operated a beach club where the nursery school is today, by the empty lifeguard tower and the closed path. The documents weren't random. They were the archives of someone who cared. She had handwritten notes, hand-drawn parcel maps, pristine copies of the city's own documents, sitting in my garage since the seventies. She was an architecture student. She had her house designed by a fellow classmate, Doug Rucker, AIA. It was his first professional commission.

So I took all the old documents from the garage and started scanning them through the feeder. One after the next. Not really paying attention, just ripping out pages and feeding them in. Anything that looked interesting. I found the old bylaws. I found how they had been restated. I wondered why they had been restated. Nobody had ever said why. They just talked about how much effort had gone into it.

I used AI to figure out that the bylaws had been restated in 2009 because regulations required HOAs to remain compliant with the law if they were going to take any enforcement action. And so our community executed a full quorum vote to amend and restate the deed restrictions, the last time the community actually achieved that, which was quite a feat. But nobody said the real reason. They said it was important to remain compliant. The real reason was that there was exclusionary language in the original founding documents. You had to be white. In order to remain compliant with the law, the HOA had to remove that language. Nobody talked about it. They just did it.

And then I found the founding declaration. And I saw the name on it. Kelvin C. Vanderlip, President, Palos Verdes Corporation. This wasn't just any HOA. This was the HOA that the Vanderlip family created for their own estate. The family that helped create the Federal Reserve. The family that hired the Olmsted Brothers to plan the entire peninsula. They signed both sides of the document; president of the land corporation and president of the HOA at the same time. The same people, creating the restrictions and the body to enforce them.

Once I understood what the AI could do with these documents, I started feeding it everything. Seventy-five-year-old declarations recorded in books at the Los Angeles County Recorder's Office. It read them. The faded typewriter text, the legal descriptions, the metes and bounds. I asked it to compare legal descriptions across documents, and it found matches I would have missed. I gave it engineering drawings and assessor parcel maps, and it helped me trace boundary lines from one document to another.

The Lot H Declaration was instructive, but not in the way I expected. The AI helped me see the pattern: the Vanderlips put declarations on the parcels they were selling. Lot H covered the negative space. But when I read the actual document, I discovered that the only restriction it contained was a racial covenant, now void. The architectural protections I assumed were there simply weren't. That correction, documented in our research log, changed the entire legal strategy. The strongest arguments for protecting this land are geological, environmental, and procedural, not CC&R-based.

But the real discovery came from the engineering drawings. I was looking at county surveys of Palos Verdes Drive South from the 1930s, one of the most surveyed pieces of road in the world, it turns out, and the AI pointed out a map document reference I would have missed. A book and page number in tiny notation on a thirty-by-forty-inch blueprint. I pulled the referenced document from the county recorder. It pointed to two declarations I had never heard anyone mention: Declaration 100 and Declaration 101.

They were written on the same typewriter, by the same legal assistant, on the same letterhead, and signed by the same people who signed everything else the Vanderlips recorded. They defined a Palos Verdes Community Association and an Art Jury, not an architectural review committee, an Art Jury, the same body that governs Palos Verdes Estates today. The same red tile district. The same Mediterranean architectural standards. All for Portuguese Bend. I don't think anybody has ever publicly discussed these documents. They never mattered because the land was always private. But when the Filiorum Corporation dissolved and the family's speculation ended, I believe these restrictions may still apply to the nooks and crannies left behind.

I used GIS mapping tools to plot the seven parcels described in Declaration 101. The metes and bounds are partially illegible on the microfilm. The AI flagged the uncertain readings and I marked them as draft. I overlaid the declaration boundaries on modern assessor parcel maps. The lot at 0 Clipper Road sits just outside the Declaration 101 boundary. You can actually see the lot line in the original Olmsted drawings. The result is in the interactive map on this site.

What one person can do now with these tools used to require a law firm, a title company, and a surveyor. I'm not claiming the work is perfect. The microfilm quality is poor on some of the oldest documents, and there are gaps I haven't filled. But the timeline is assembled, the documents are indexed, and the contradictions in the public record are visible to anyone who wants to look.

The Absurdity

Drive Palos Verdes Drive South past the fire station in the spring of 2026. The road is cracked. City works signs are broken and leaning. Flashing electronic signs warn of dangerous conditions. The Governor declared a state of emergency for this area in September 2024. FEMA has been involved. The ground beneath the fire station, which sits on land carved from Parcel 106, the same remainder strip that borders the vacant lot, is moving.

The fire station is in a high fire zone. The driveway entrance is dangerous. This is not a contested claim. This is what you see when you drive past it.

And the city wants to put sixteen units here.

The HOA, the governance structure that the Vanderlips built in 1949 to protect this land, can't function. Dues are less than twenty dollars a month. That budget can't fund legal action against a developer backed by a two-million-dollar loan. When the board tries to enforce the CC&Rs, individual board members get personally named in lawsuits. They need directors and officers insurance that a volunteer board with a shoestring budget can't afford. When neighbors dispute, the board can't intervene without exposing itself.

The state tells HOAs they can't restrict development. The courts say CC&Rs still have teeth. The city uses the restrictions as an obstacle when it doesn't want to spend money. It won't fix the beach path, probably because the CC&Rs encumber the parking lot, but ignores them when a developer wants to build next door.

The city is happy to cite residents. Two-hundred-and-fifty-dollar tickets for a gardener's leaf blower. Off-leash citations in a park the city itself closed.

But sixteen units on an active landslide? Hands are tied. And the land conservancy, adjacent to the whole issue? Silent on the matter. What butterfly? What butterfly.

Why?

The Shore Club vote was in 1972. The Coastal Specific Plan was adopted in 1978. The moratorium ordinance the same year. The Wong subdivision in 1980. The previous owners tried and were told no. Every generation on this coastline has fought the same fight, and every generation has won, because the land itself makes the argument. You cannot build here. The geology says no. The hydrology says no. A hundred years of recorded instruments say no. The city's own engineers say no.

The California Housing Element says yes. A planning department staffed by people who don't live here says yes. A consulting firm that presented to the planning commission in April 2025 said this was just about numbers, that nobody would ever actually need permits. If that's true, then the rezoning is theater. But someone has a two-million-dollar loan on this parcel. Someone intends to build.

The housing element was designed for infill corridors. Jefferson Boulevard in West LA, La Cienega, Pleasanton in the East Bay. Places where density makes sense, where infrastructure exists, where the ground doesn't move. Not for condos on the beach on an active landslide in a high fire zone next to a failing fire station on a road the city can't maintain.

The Vanderlips built a system to prevent this. Declarations. Art Juries. Architectural committees. CC&Rs that run with the land and renew automatically every ten years. They thought of everything. But they didn't anticipate a state government that would override all of it. SB 9 is probably the one California development loophole the Vanderlips didn't invent.

At the end of the day, everyone in this story has always believed they had the right. The city. The state. The HOA. The developer. The individual property owner. In every chapter, someone has felt justified. But there is one party whose claim isn't a matter of opinion or legal interpretation. The El Segundo Blue Butterfly is a federally endangered species. Its habitat is on this parcel. The Endangered Species Act doesn't care about housing elements or planning commissions or spreadsheets. It trumps all of it.

When it's convenient, you use the regulation that matters most. In this case, it might be the butterfly that gets the last say.

So here is what we think should happen. Revert to the 1980 Wong subdivision. Honor the flood hazard designation. Preserve the 0.57 acres of open space, the butterfly habitat, as a park. The remaining four single-family residential lots, zoned RS-4 as they were always intended, are consistent with the neighborhood. They are compatible with the area. They can be built if the land is stable enough.

The record is right there. The declarations. The geological surveys. The city's own coastal plan. The moratorium. The emergency declaration. The court filings. We put it all in the evidence room because we think you should be able to read it yourself.

And so we created a 501(c)(3) to prevent them from paving this piece of paradise.
